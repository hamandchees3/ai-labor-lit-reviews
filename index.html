AI Labor-Market Policy Proposal

Background evidence for a market-oriented “technology adjustment assistance” program · Hammond & Manning

A labor-market policy proposal for addressing AI and other technology shocks, by Samuel Hammond and Sam Manning. The work is organized as two sections that can stand alone or combine into a white paper: a conceptual motivation and a policy proposal for a market-oriented “technology adjustment assistance” program that targets labor demand directly.

The central claim is that AI’s labor impact is best understood not as a uniform jobs apocalypse but as a reallocation shock: demand collapses in some occupations and surges in others, with no frictionless path for displaced workers to fill the rising roles. The standard toolkit answers this badly — federal workforce programs are fragmented, classroom retraining detached from employment shows weak returns, and the leading political alternative (a government job guarantee) is the design the evidence ranks least effective. The seven reviews below assemble the evidence base behind that argument.

Section I · Conceptual motivation

Frames the AI labor shock as reallocation and shows why the existing toolkit and the job-guarantee alternative misfit it. Draws chiefly on reviews 01, 02, 04, 05.

Section II · Policy proposal

A worker-following employment subsidy that targets demand, ties training to real jobs, and builds in automatic stabilizers. Draws chiefly on reviews 03, 06.

Conceptual motivationReview 01

01 — AI’s Labor Impact as a Reallocation Shock

Abstract

The dominant public framing of artificial intelligence’s labor-market impact oscillates between two poles: an imminent “net-jobs apocalypse” in which automation erases work faster than it creates it, and a reassuring “lump-of-labor fallacy” rebuttal in which aggregate employment is unaffected because new jobs always appear. This review argues that both framings obscure the policy-relevant reality. The better-supported characterization, grounded in four decades of labor economics and the first wave of empirical AI studies, is that AI is a dislocation and reallocation shock: it lowers labor demand in some occupations and tasks while raising it in others, and the workers shed from contracting roles cannot frictionlessly move into expanding ones because of skill, geographic, and information frictions. The task-based framework of Autor, Levy, and Murnane [AutorLevyMurnane2003] and the polarization literature [AutorDorn2013; Autor2015] supply the theoretical spine. Recent evidence sharpens the picture: early-career workers in highly AI-exposed occupations show roughly a 13% relative employment decline [Brynjolfsson2025], even as macro-level wage and hours effects remain near zero in the near term [Humlum2025; Acemoglu2024]. Bureau of Labor Statistics projections for 2024–34 show clerical, administrative, and routine-cognitive occupations declining while skilled trades, clean-energy installation, and healthcare support roles grow [BLS2025]. The aggregate may be modest; the distributional churn is not. This asymmetry is the conceptual ground for targeted, demand-side adjustment policy rather than aggregate stimulus or undifferentiated income support.

§1 — Framing

Whether AI’s labor impact is a “net-loss” problem or a “reallocation” problem is not a semantic quibble. It determines which policy instrument is appropriate.

If one believes AI will produce a net destruction of jobs — fewer total positions than workers seeking them — then the logical response is some form of aggregate demand management or universal income floor: macroeconomic stimulus, a universal basic income, or a government job guarantee that absorbs surplus labor. The diagnosis is scarcity of work in the aggregate, so the cure is to manufacture or subsidize aggregate work or aggregate income.

If, instead, AI is a reallocation shock — total labor demand roughly preserved but redistributed across occupations, sectors, and places — then aggregate instruments are poorly matched to the problem. Stimulus does not help a displaced insurance-claims processor become a wind-turbine technician; an income floor cushions the fall but does nothing to clear the mismatch between where labor is shed and where it is demanded. The binding constraint is not the quantity of work but the transition between kinds of work, and the frictions — retraining, relocation, information, and credentialing — that make that transition slow, costly, and incomplete.

The historical record favors the reallocation reading. Mechanized agriculture, containerized shipping, and the “China shock” of import competition did not produce permanent mass unemployment; they produced localized, occupationally concentrated, and often generationally persistent dislocation [Autor2015; AutorDorn2013]. Aggregate employment recovered; specific communities and cohorts frequently did not. The lesson is that aggregate statistics can look benign while the distribution underneath is violently churning. A policy designed around the aggregate will systematically miss the people the shock actually lands on.

This framing also disciplines the rhetoric. It resists both the alarmism that predicts mass technological unemployment (a prediction the data do not yet support) and the complacency that treats any concern as a lump-of-labor fallacy (a complacency that ignores the real, measurable, concentrated harm to displaced workers). The reallocation framing takes seriously that the costs of adjustment are real and unevenly borne even when the aggregate is fine — and that this is precisely the case for a targeted, demand-side policy that helps labor demand find displaced workers rather than waiting for displaced workers to find demand.

§2 — Seminal foundations

The intellectual foundation for the reallocation view is the task-based framework of technological change, which displaced the older “skill-biased technical change” account by reframing the unit of analysis from workers to the tasks that compose a job.

The canonical statement is Autor, Levy, and Murnane’s “The Skill Content of Recent Technological Change” [AutorLevyMurnane2003]. Their insight: computer capital substitutes for labor in tasks that can be expressed as explicit, codifiable rules (“routine” tasks, whether cognitive or manual), and complements labor in tasks requiring non-routine problem-solving or complex interpersonal communication. The empirical signature is a within-industry, within-occupation decline in routine-task labor input and a rise in non-routine cognitive input; their model accounts for roughly 60% of the estimated relative demand shift toward college-educated labor over 1970–1998. Crucially, the framework is task-level, not job-level: a single occupation is a bundle of tasks, some automatable and some not, so technology reshapes jobs from within rather than simply deleting or preserving them whole.

This task lens generates the job polarization prediction, documented by Autor and Dorn [AutorDorn2013] and synthesized in Autor’s widely cited overview [Autor2015] and his Brookings paper with Katz and Kearney [Autor2010]. Because routine tasks cluster in middle-wage occupations (clerical, administrative, repetitive production), automation hollows out the middle of the wage distribution while leaving high-skill non-routine cognitive work and low-skill non-routine manual service work relatively intact — producing a “U-shaped” growth pattern across the wage distribution. Polarization is the macro-scale fingerprint of task-level reallocation, and it is inherently a story about which jobs grow and shrink, not about the aggregate count.

Two implications carry directly into the AI era. First, the relevant distinction is automation versus augmentation: technology that substitutes for a worker’s tasks reduces labor demand for that role, while technology that complements a worker’s tasks raises it. The same general-purpose tool can do both, in different proportions, across different occupations. Second, because the effect operates task-by-task, displacement and creation occur simultaneously and in different places — the precise condition that makes frictionless reallocation impossible and adjustment policy necessary. Autor’s later work on the geography of opportunity [Autor2019] reinforces that these frictions are spatial as well as occupational: the routine-intensive labor markets hit hardest were often those least able to generate replacement work locally.

§3 — Recent evidence (2023–2026)

The arrival of large language models after late 2022 has produced the first wave of direct empirical evidence on AI’s labor effects. Read together, these studies confirm the reallocation pattern: real, measurable displacement concentrated in specific occupations and cohorts, set against modest aggregate effects in the near term.

Concentrated displacement among the young and the exposed. The most striking early evidence comes from Brynjolfsson, Chandar, and Chen’s “Canaries in the Coal Mine?” [Brynjolfsson2025], which uses high-frequency, individual-level payroll data from ADP, the largest U.S. payroll processor. Their headline finding: early-career workers aged 22–25 in the most AI-exposed occupations experienced roughly a 13% relative decline in employment since generative AI’s diffusion, even after controlling for firm-level shocks. The effect is concentrated where AI automates rather than augments tasks, adjusts primarily through employment (headcount) rather than compensation, and is far weaker or absent for older workers in the same occupations — consistent with firms slowing entry-level hiring where AI substitutes for the tasks junior workers traditionally performed. This is reallocation in its sharpest empirical form: not an across-the-board jobs collapse, but a precise withdrawal of demand at one margin (young workers, automatable tasks) while the rest of the labor market continues largely undisturbed.

Near-zero aggregate effects, so far. Counterbalancing the displacement evidence, Humlum and Vestergaard’s “Large Language Models, Small Labor Market Effects” [Humlum2025] links two large adoption surveys (2023–2024, ~25,000 workers across 11 exposed occupations) to Danish administrative employer-employee records. Using difference-in-differences, they find precise null effects on earnings and recorded hours at both worker and workplace levels — ruling out effects larger than ~2% in the two years after ChatGPT’s launch — despite widespread adoption. Average time savings were only about 3%. Notably, they do document task reorganization, new AI-related job tasks, and some worker transitions toward higher-paying occupations — reallocation activity without (yet) net earnings movement. The NBER working-paper version of this work appears under the title “Still Waters, Rapid Currents” [HumlumVestergaard2025nber].

Usage patterns: augmentation roughly matching automation. Anthropic’s Economic Index [Anthropic2025] maps roughly one million anonymized Claude.ai conversations onto the ~20,000 tasks in the Department of Labor’s ONET taxonomy. The February 2025 baseline found 57% of usage classified as augmentation and 43% as automation* — i.e., AI is used about as often to assist a worker as to perform a task outright. Usage is heavily concentrated in mid-to-high-wage cognitive occupations (Computer and Mathematical tasks alone were ~37% of conversations despite that group being ~3.4% of the workforce), and is low at both the bottom and the very top of the wage distribution. Later updates show the augmentation share continuing to edge above half. The index is a usage measure, not an employment measure, but it directly operationalizes the automation-versus-augmentation distinction at the heart of the reallocation framing: AI’s labor effect will be the net of where it substitutes and where it complements, and those are different tasks in different jobs.

The declining-versus-rising-demand split in the official projections. The Bureau of Labor Statistics Employment Projections for 2024–34 [BLS2025] make the reallocation structure explicit at the occupational level.

  • Declining. The fastest-declining occupations in percentage terms are routine-cognitive and clerical: clerical/word-processor typists (projected ~−36%), telephone operators (~−28%), receptionists (~−26%), and data-entry keyers (~−26%). In absolute terms, cashiers lose the most positions (~−314,000, about −10%), and general office clerks decline ~7%. BLS attributes these declines explicitly to automation and the expanding integration of AI into office and administrative workflows. Routine sales and many administrative-support and routine design tasks are similarly constrained.
  • Rising. The fastest-growing occupations are clean-energy and care roles: wind turbine service technicians (~+50%, the single fastest-growing occupation BLS tracks) and solar photovoltaic installers (~+42%), though both add fewer than ~20,000 jobs combined because they start from small bases. Healthcare and social assistance is the largest-growth and fastest-growing industry sector (~+8.4%), with nurse practitioners, physician assistants, and physical-therapist assistants among the fastest-growing occupations; data scientists and information-security analysts also rank near the top.

The shape is unmistakable: demand falls in routine office/clerical/sales work and rises in skilled installation, the care economy, and specific high-skill technical fields. A laid-off data-entry keyer in a metro office market is not a ready substitute for a wind-turbine technician on a rural wind farm or a nurse practitioner requiring a graduate clinical credential. The occupational, geographic, and credentialing distance between the shrinking and growing roles is the friction the policy must address.

On the “skilled trades in high demand” claim. The team’s working intuition that skilled trades such as electricians are in strong demand is directionally supported but should be stated precisely. Per the BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook, electricians had a median annual wage of $62,350 in May 2024 (lowest 10% < $39,430; highest 10% > $106,030), with employment projected to grow 9% from 2024 to 2034 (“much faster than average”) and about 81,000 openings per year on average over the decade, many from replacement needs [BLSElectricians2024]. Electricians are a genuinely strong, growing, replacement-heavy trade — but they are not among the BLS top-ranked fastest-growing occupations (those are wind/solar/healthcare roles above), and the median wage is in the low-$60,000s, not anywhere near six figures at the median. The widely circulated “$300,000 electrician jobs” figure is not supported by BLS data; it likely reflects rare outliers (overtime-heavy union work in high-cost metros, specialized industrial or owner-operator roles) rather than typical pay. We therefore cite the verified BLS figures and flag the anecdote as [speculative — anecdotal, not BLS-confirmed].

§4 — Counter-evidence & caveats

The reallocation framing should be held with appropriate humility. Several lines of evidence and reasoning cut against an aggressive reading.

The modest-macro view. Acemoglu’s “The Simple Macroeconomics of AI” [Acemoglu2024] is the most prominent skeptical anchor. Starting from a task-based model and applying a Hulten-theorem accounting of task-level cost savings, he estimates that AI will raise total factor productivity by no more than ~0.66% over ten years — and likely under ~0.53% once harder-to-automate tasks are accounted for. On this view, AI’s near-term aggregate effects, including on labor, are real but small, and the bulk of adjustment occurs “at the margin” through gradual task reallocation rather than through dramatic, economy-wide displacement. A complementary firm-level study, Hampole, Papanikolaou, Schmidt, and Seegmiller’s “Artificial Intelligence and the Labor Market” [Hampole2025] (NBER w33509), finds productivity gains from AI adoption but limited or insignificant aggregate labor-market impacts to date. The Humlum–Vestergaard null results [Humlum2025] point the same way. If these views are right, the pace of dislocation may be slow enough that ordinary labor-market turnover absorbs much of it.

Measurement and identification limits. The displacement studies face genuine inference challenges. The 2022–2025 window overlaps with post-pandemic labor-market normalization, sectoral interest-rate shocks (notably in tech), and a hiring pullback that is hard to disentangle from AI per se. “AI exposure” is typically an occupational measure of task overlap with AI capabilities, not a measure of actual deployment, so exposure-based estimates can conflate AI with other correlated shocks. Reported point estimates for the Brynjolfsson young-worker effect vary (commonly ~13%, but up to ~16% depending on controls), and the work is recent and not yet peer-reviewed in final form. BLS projections, for their part, are model-based extrapolations that have historically under-predicted some technology disruptions and over-predicted others; they are a structured judgment, not a forecast with confidence intervals.

Uncertainty about pace and capability trajectory. The deepest caveat is that all of this evidence is an early snapshot of a fast-moving capability frontier. The augmentation-heavy usage pattern today [Anthropic2025] could shift toward automation as agentic systems mature; conversely, complementarities could deepen and raise labor demand in augmented roles. The reallocation framing does not depend on knowing the pace — it holds whether AI’s diffusion is slow or fast — but the urgency and scale of any policy response do depend on it, and that pace is genuinely uncertain.

Importantly, none of these caveats overturns the reallocation thesis; they bound its magnitude and timing. Acemoglu’s own model is a task-reallocation model — his disagreement is about size, not structure. A modest aggregate effect fully co-exists with concentrated, painful, occupationally and geographically specific dislocation, because aggregates average over exactly the distribution the displaced worker lives in. The policy case rests on the distributional claim, which the skeptical literature does not contest.

What this implies for the conceptual motivation section

The evidence assembled here converges on a single structural claim: AI is best understood as a reallocation shock, not a net-jobs apocalypse. Labor demand falls in routine clerical, administrative, and sales work and rises in skilled trades, clean-energy installation, healthcare, and select high-skill technical fields — and the workers shed by the former cannot frictionlessly flow into the latter because of skill, credential, geographic, and information barriers. The aggregate effects may be modest [Acemoglu2024; Humlum2025]; the distributional churn, concentrated on specific cohorts and occupations [Brynjolfsson2025; BLS2025], is not.

This diagnosis is the hinge of the conceptual motivation section, because it rules out two intuitive but mismatched responses. Aggregate stimulus treats a quantity-of-work problem the labor market does not actually have. Pure income support (UBI-style floors, or a government job guarantee that manufactures aggregate employment) cushions the fall but does nothing to clear the mismatch between where labor is shed and where it is demanded — and the worst-evidenced of these, public employment-of-last-resort programs, are addressed in the policy review. If the problem is a transition that frictions make slow, costly, and incomplete, the right instrument is one that acts on the transition itself: a targeted, demand-side technology adjustment assistance approach that lowers the cost of moving displaced workers into roles where demand is rising and supply is short — letting wages and price signals identify those roles rather than central planners picking winners. The reallocation framing is precisely what makes a demand-following adjustment program, rather than aggregate stimulus or undifferentiated income support, the analytically correct response.

02 — WIOA and the Fragmentation of US Workforce Policy

Abstract

The United States lacks a coherent active labor-market policy (ALMP). What it has instead is a patchwork of dozens of separately authorized, separately funded employment and training programs spread across many federal agencies, loosely stitched together at the front end by the One-Stop / American Job Center system. The Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act of 2014 (WIOA) is the closest thing to a unifying statute, organizing six “core” programs under four titles split between the Department of Labor and the Department of Education, requiring Unified State Plans, and imposing a common set of performance indicators. But WIOA governs only a slice of the federal workforce-development enterprise. The Government Accountability Office’s recurring inventories — 47 programs across nine agencies spending roughly $18 billion in fiscal year 2009 (GAO-11-92), still 43 programs across nine agencies in 2019 (GAO-19-200) — document persistent fragmentation, near-universal service overlap, and a near-total absence of rigorous impact evidence (only five of the 47 programs had a completed impact study since 2004). Performance data are themselves unreliable and hard to integrate across systems (GAO-15-764R, GAO-16-287). Reauthorization has repeatedly stalled, most recently when a bipartisan, bicameral agreement (A Stronger Workforce for America Act) was dropped from a December 2024 continuing resolution. This review argues that the diagnosis is not “underfunding” but architecture: the system works through a fragmented bureaucracy and a supply-side training paradigm rather than targeting labor demand, and is too tangled to retrofit. That motivates a technology adjustment assistance program designed to overlay existing systems rather than reform them from within.

§1 — Framing: why “fragmentation” is the right diagnosis

A serviceable active labor-market policy does a small number of things well. It helps displaced workers find the jobs that exist (matching and placement); where the worker’s prior skills no longer clear the market, it finances retraining toward occupations where demand is rising; it provides income support during the transition; and — crucially — it does these things through a channel a displaced worker or a hiring employer can actually find and use. Implicit in this is a demand-side function: an ALMP should make it cheaper or easier for employers to hire and retain the displaced, not merely process job seekers through a service menu. The OECD’s taxonomy of ALMPs (training, employment incentives/hiring subsidies, public employment services, direct job creation, sheltered employment) is the standard reference point, and the United States is a conspicuous low spender on all of them as a share of GDP [OECD2023].

Against that yardstick, the central problem with US workforce policy is not that any single program is badly run. It is that there is no “system” in any meaningful sense — only an accretion of programs, each the residue of a particular political moment, a particular target population, and a particular committee jurisdiction. “Fragmentation” is the right word precisely because the dysfunction is structural and emergent rather than localized. The GAO uses three terms of art that map onto this diagnosis [GAO2011a]: fragmentation (more than one federal agency, or more than one organization within an agency, involved in the same broad area of national need), overlap (multiple programs offering similar services to similar populations), and duplication (two or more programs engaged in the same activities to serve the same beneficiaries). The federal employment-and-training enterprise exhibits all three, and has for decades.

The consequence is a navigation problem that falls hardest on the people the system exists to serve. A laid-off worker confronts not one door but many — and the One-Stop system, designed to consolidate those doors, sits on top of programs that retain their own eligibility rules, funding streams, performance metrics, and administering agencies. An employer that might hire and train displaced workers faces a still more opaque landscape. The system’s energy is absorbed by coordination: aligning plans, harmonizing data, negotiating cost-sharing among co-located partners. That is overhead spent on managing fragmentation rather than on closing the gap between a displaced worker and a job. This is the core of the conceptual-motivation argument: the binding constraint is architecture, not appropriation. A program designed for a discrete, demand-side reallocation shock should not be threaded into this machinery; it should overlay it.

§2 — The architecture of WIOA

WIOA (P.L. 113-128), enacted July 22, 2014, is the principal federal statute for workforce development. It superseded the Workforce Investment Act of 1998 (WIA, P.L. 105-220), which had itself replaced the Job Training Partnership Act of 1982 — a lineage of consolidation attempts running back through the Comprehensive Employment and Training Act of 1973 [Wikipedia_WIA; CharlotteWorks]. Each successor statute promised greater coordination; each left most of the surrounding program ecology in place.

The six core programs. WIOA organizes six core programs under four titles, administered jointly by two cabinet departments [CRS_R44252; DOL_WIOAprograms]:

  • Title I (Department of Labor): the Adult, Dislocated Worker, and Youth formula programs — three of the six core programs, and the part of WIOA most associated with “job training.”
  • Title II (Department of Education): the Adult Education and Family Literacy Act (AEFLA) program.
  • Title III (Department of Labor): the Wagner-Peyser Act Employment Service, the public labor-exchange program dating to 1933, as amended by WIOA.
  • Title IV (Department of Education): the Vocational Rehabilitation State Grants program, authorized under the Rehabilitation Act of 1973.

That a single “unifying” statute splits its six core programs across two departments — Labor running Titles I and III, Education running Titles II and IV — is itself emblematic. Coordination must happen across a cabinet-level seam.

The One-Stop / American Job Center delivery system. WIOA’s signature integration mechanism is the One-Stop delivery system, branded since 2015 as the American Job Center (AJC) network. The premise is single-door access: a job seeker should be able to reach the full menu of employment-related services in one physical (and increasingly virtual) location. Each local area must operate at least one comprehensive One-Stop center. But the AJC is a front-end overlay, not a merger of the underlying programs. Beyond the six core programs, WIOA designates a set of required one-stop partner programs — additional federally funded programs (e.g., TANF employment activities, Job Corps, the Senior Community Service Employment Program, Trade Adjustment Assistance, veterans’ employment services, Carl D. Perkins career-and-technical education, Community Services Block Grant employment activities, HUD employment programs, and Unemployment Insurance) that must make their services available through the center and contribute proportionately to its costs [CRS_R44252; DOL_AJC]. The number is commonly cited as the six core programs plus roughly a dozen additional required partners. Each partner retains its own statutory authority, eligibility criteria, and accountability chain; the One-Stop coordinates access to them rather than absorbing them.

Governance: Unified State Plans and boards. WIOA requires each state to submit a Unified State Plan (or a Combined State Plan that folds in additional partner programs) covering the six core programs for a four-year cycle, jointly approved by Labor and Education [CRS_R44252]. Service delivery is governed by state and local Workforce Development Boards with mandated business-majority membership, and funds flow by statutory formula to local workforce areas. The governance layer is genuinely federated — which is a strength for local responsiveness and a weakness for coherence, since it multiplies the number of plans, boards, and reporting relationships.

Performance accountability. A core WIOA innovation was a common set of primary performance indicators across the six core programs: employment in the second and fourth quarters after exit, median earnings, credential attainment, measurable skill gains, and an effectiveness-in-serving-employers indicator [CRS_R44252; DOL_perfindicators]. The intent was to make programs comparable and to enable integrated data systems. As §3 shows, the implementation has fallen well short of the design.

Funding. WIOA’s core Title I formula programs are modestly funded relative to the scale of US labor-market churn. For Program Year 2024, the Title I state formula allotments were roughly $3.9 billion — about $886 million for Adult activities, $1.40 billion for Dislocated Worker activities, and $948 million for Youth activities — appropriated through the Further Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2024 (P.L. 118-47) [FedReg_PY2024; DOL_PY2024allot]. These figures have been essentially flat to declining in real terms for years [TCF2024]. Even adding the Wagner-Peyser Employment Service and the Education-administered titles, total WIOA funding is a small fraction of what the United States spends on, say, the social safety net, and a small fraction of OECD peers’ ALMP spending as a share of GDP [OECD2023]. The point for the motivation section is not merely that the pot is small, but that it is carved up: a few billion dollars of discretionary training money, distributed by formula across thousands of local areas and braided with a dozen partner programs, is structurally incapable of mounting a targeted response to a concentrated demand shock.

§3 — The fragmentation and overlap evidence

The empirical backbone of the fragmentation diagnosis is the GAO’s recurring inventory of federal employment-and-training programs, reinforced by its audits of WIOA performance data.

The classic count: 47 programs, nine agencies, ~$18 billion (GAO-11-92). In January 2011, GAO reported that in fiscal year 2009 the federal government operated 47 employment and training programs administered across nine agencies, spending approximately $18 billion [GAO2011a]. The nine agencies were the Departments of Agriculture, Defense, Education, Health and Human Services, Interior, Justice, Labor, and Veterans Affairs, plus the Environmental Protection Agency. GAO found that all but a handful of these programs overlapped with at least one other program by providing similar services to similar populations — and that only five of the 47 programs had had an impact study completed since 2004 to determine whether outcomes were actually caused by the program rather than by other factors. The fiscal-year-2009 total reflected an increase of three programs and roughly $5 billion over GAO’s 2003 inventory, partly attributable to temporary Recovery Act funding flowing to 14 of the 47 programs. The 47-program / nine-agency figure became the canonical shorthand for federal workforce fragmentation, repeated in subsequent congressional testimony [GAO2011b]. (Some secondary sources cite “44 programs,” reflecting a narrower cut of the same inventory; the headline figure GAO itself foregrounds is 47.)

The count persists: 43 programs, nine agencies (GAO-19-200). Eight years and one major reauthorization later, GAO returned to the question. In March 2019 it reported that the count had fallen only marginally — from 47 in 2011 to 43 employment and training programs, still spanning nine agencies [GAO2019]. The programs continued to overlap heavily: 39 of 43 offered employment counseling and assessment, 38 of 43 offered job-readiness training. Almost all (38 of 43) reported at least one action to manage fragmentation or overlap — co-location, information sharing — but agencies could not consistently report the results of those actions, and few evaluations spanned multiple programs. GAO recommended that Labor develop a strategic plan for evaluating these programs. The persistence of the count through the WIA-to-WIOA transition is the single most telling datum for the motivation argument: a statute explicitly billed as consolidation removed, on net, only a few programs from a 47-program landscape.

Performance-data weakness (GAO-15-764R, GAO-16-287). WIOA’s common-measures framework was supposed to make the system legible. GAO’s early implementation audits found otherwise. GAO-15-764R (2015) flagged performance-reporting and related challenges as WIOA stood up [GAO2015]. GAO-16-287 (March 2016) documented that, in the states GAO examined (Illinois, New Hampshire, Texas), implementation was slowed by incomplete regulations and unresolved reporting details; that missing participant data threatened the quality of the information states report to federal agencies; that some states resorted to participant surveys to capture employment outcomes because state privacy laws impeded wage-record matching; and that integrating the data systems behind the common indicators was costly and complex, with resource constraints cited as a binding obstacle [GAO2016]. In other words, even the measurement layer designed to impose coherence is fragmented and unreliable. A system that cannot consistently say whether its programs work, or compare them on common terms, cannot be steered.

Synthesis of the evidence. Three findings recur across two decades of GAO work and survive the WIOA reform: (1) the program count and agency spread are large and sticky; (2) overlap in services to similar populations is near-universal; and (3) rigorous impact evidence is almost entirely absent, and the performance data that exist are unreliable and siloed. The reasonable inference is that fragmentation is a stable equilibrium of the US workforce-policy architecture, not a transient condition awaiting the next reauthorization.

§4 — Counter-evidence and caveats

Intellectual honesty requires registering the case against the fragmentation framing, and the genuine merits of the existing system.

WIOA was, in fact, a consolidation — and accomplished real things. WIOA did streamline relative to WIA: it reduced the number of required common performance indicators into a single aligned set across the six core programs, mandated Unified State Plans, strengthened the business-led board structure, and emphasized sector strategies and career pathways [CRS_R44252]. The One-Stop model genuinely does give many job seekers a single point of entry, and the system serves millions of people annually at low per-capita cost. The marginal drop in GAO’s program count (47 to 43) understates qualitative integration at the front end. Defenders argue, not unreasonably, that some “fragmentation” is just federalism plus pluralism — different programs serve genuinely different populations (veterans, people with disabilities, dislocated workers, youth, the limited-English-proficient), and forcing them into one mold would sacrifice tailoring for tidiness [TCF2024]. GAO’s own framing is careful: overlap is not the same as wasteful duplication, and co-location can capture efficiencies without merger [GAO2011a; GAO2019].

The evidence on training efficacy is mixed, not uniformly damning. The near-absence of impact studies cuts both ways: it means we cannot confidently say the programs fail, only that we cannot say they succeed. Where rigorous evidence exists, sector-based training programs (e.g., Year Up, Project QUEST, WorkAdvance) show meaningful earnings effects, suggesting the problem is less “training doesn’t work” than “most programs are not designed or evaluated like the ones that work.” (This is the subject of a companion review on retraining efficacy; flagged here only as a caveat.) [speculative — the precise read of the training-efficacy literature is developed in the companion review, not here.]

Reform is being attempted — but keeps stalling. The system is not frozen by indifference. The A Stronger Workforce for America Act (SWFA / ASWA) would reauthorize and amend WIOA: the House passed it 378-26 in April 2024 (H.R. 6655, 118th Congress), and in November 2024 House and Senate committee leaders announced a bipartisan, bicameral agreement [CRS_R47905; NAWB_agreement]. It was then dropped from the December 2024 continuing resolution at the last minute and did not pass before the 118th Congress adjourned [NLC2024; CCDaily2024]. It was reintroduced in the 119th Congress (H.R. 8210, 2025-26) and, as of this writing, has not been enacted [Congress_HR8210]. The pattern — broad bipartisan support, repeated near-misses, no enactment — is itself evidence for the motivation argument: even consensus reform of this architecture is extraordinarily hard to land, because it must reconcile dozens of stakeholders, jurisdictions, and funding streams. A program that depends on first fixing WIOA is a program that may wait a decade.

The honest residual. None of these caveats overturns the core diagnosis. They refine it. The right reading is not “WIOA is worthless” but “WIOA is the best available attempt to coordinate an irreducibly fragmented system, and even at its best it is a coordination layer over programs it cannot merge, measured by data it cannot trust, and it cannot mount a targeted, demand-side response to a concentrated dislocation shock.” That is a structural ceiling, not a funding gap.

What this implies for the conceptual motivation section

The fragmentation evidence licenses a specific design posture for a technology adjustment assistance program. First, do not retrofit. The system is too fragmented, too jurisdictionally entangled, and too slow to reform — three decades of GAO findings and a serially stalled reauthorization show that working through WIOA’s machinery would subordinate a demand shock response to the system’s coordination overhead and political veto points. Second, overlay rather than replace. The existing One-Stop infrastructure, state workforce boards, and wage-record systems are real assets; a new program should sit on top of them — using them as delivery rails where convenient — without inheriting their fragmented funding streams or eligibility rules. Federal matching dollars can flow through this overlay and let states reallocate existing workforce funds toward it, rather than standing up a parallel bureaucracy. Third, target demand directly. The deepest structural flaw in the legacy system is that it is overwhelmingly supply-side: it processes, counsels, and trains job seekers, but does little to make displaced workers cheaper or more attractive to hire. A worker-following employment subsidy inverts this — it attaches resources to the displaced worker and pays out when an employer actually hires and retains them, converting a service-delivery problem into a price signal. Fourth, build in measurement from the start. Given that the legacy system’s defining evidentiary failure is the near-total absence of credible impact data and the unreliability of its performance reporting, a new program should be architected around clean, auditable outcomes (employment and earnings via wage records, with a credible counterfactual) so that, unlike its predecessors, it can actually be evaluated. In short: the fragmentation of US workforce policy is not an argument for one more program inside the patchwork — it is an argument for a demand-targeting overlay that routes around the patchwork while leveraging its rails.

Motivation + PolicyReview 03

03 — The Efficacy of Worker Retraining: Why Employer-Linked Training Outperforms

Abstract

This review assesses the empirical record on worker retraining as a response to labor-market dislocation, with an eye to what it implies for a general technology adjustment assistance program. The evidence is unusually clean for a social-policy question, because the two contrasting models have both been evaluated with large randomized controlled trials. The traditional model — generic, voucher-driven, classroom training detached from any particular job or employer, as embodied in the Workforce Investment Act (WIA) Adult and Dislocated Worker programs — produced no statistically detectable earnings gains from training in the gold-standard RCT of more than 34,000 job seekers [McConnell2016; Fortson2017]. By contrast, a cluster of sectoral, employer-linked, job-attached programs — synthesized across four RCTs by Katz, Roth, Hendra, and Schaberg — generated earnings gains of roughly 14 to 39 percent, and, unusually for the active-labor-market literature, those gains persisted for six to nine years rather than fading out [Katz2022]. The flagship cases (WorkAdvance, Year Up, Project QUEST) share a common design DNA: occupational training tied to demonstrated employer demand in a specific sector, delivery of certifiable, transferable credentials, up-front screening, wraparound supports, and — critically — a real connection to actual jobs (in Year Up’s case, a paid internship at a partner firm). The principal caveats are scale, selection, sector-dependence, cost, and a demand-side limit emphasized by recent Brookings work [Jacobs2025]: even excellent training does not, by itself, create the jobs displaced workers need to move into. The dominant design lesson for the proposed program is therefore to tie training to actual employment with a real employer — a worker-following employment subsidy paired with mandatory, employer-delivered on-the-job training, rather than detached classroom retraining funded by vouchers.

§1 — Framing: Can you retrain your way out of dislocation?

The reflexive policy answer to technological displacement is “retrain the workers.” It is bipartisan, intuitive, and politically safe: it locates the remedy in the human capital of the displaced rather than in any disruption to firms or markets, and it promises that the same workers who lost ground to a new technology can be lifted back up by acquiring new skills. The companion review on the conceptual framing treats AI’s labor impact as a dislocation and reallocation shock — demand collapses in some occupations and rises in others, with no frictionless one-to-one transition between them. If that framing is right, retraining is the mechanism that is supposed to bridge the gap: it is meant to move a worker from a shrinking occupation to a growing one.

The question this review answers is narrower and more empirical: does retraining actually do that, and under what conditions? This is not idle. The United States already spends substantial sums on retraining through a fragmented apparatus (WIOA, Trade Adjustment Assistance, Pell-funded community-college training, and dozens of categorical programs), and the standard critique of that apparatus is that it is generic, supply-driven, and weakly tied to employer demand. If the generic model does not work, then “just retrain them” is not a policy — it is a slogan that funds activity without producing outcomes. If, on the other hand, some specific kinds of training work well and durably, then the policy question becomes one of design: what distinguishes the programs that deliver large, persistent earnings gains from those that deliver nothing?

The literature gives an unusually crisp answer to that design question, and it is the organizing thesis of this review: generic classroom retraining detached from a job shows weak returns, while sectoral, employer-linked, job-attached training delivers large, sometimes durable earnings gains. The two subsequent sections establish each half of that claim with RCT evidence; §4 takes the strongest counter-arguments seriously; and the closing section draws out the design implication.

A caveat on scope: this review concerns retraining efficacy in general. The specifically weak record of Trade Adjustment Assistance (TAA) training — the closest historical analogue to a trade-/technology-shock-specific program — is treated in detail in a separate review and is only gestured at here. It points the same direction: TAA participants showed lower employment in the first years and remained underemployed roughly four years out [Jacobs2025].

§2 — The weak record of detached classroom retraining

The strongest evidence on the traditional model comes from the WIA Adult and Dislocated Worker “Gold Standard” Evaluation, conducted by Mathematica for the U.S. Department of Labor [McConnell2016; Fortson2017]. This is among the largest and most rigorous workforce RCTs ever fielded: more than 34,000 job seekers across more than 200 American Job Centers in 28 local workforce areas, with random assignment (between November 2011 and April 2013) to three service tiers — “core” services only; “core-and-intensive” (adding staff-assisted, one-on-one job search and counseling); and “full-WIA,” which additionally made participants eligible for funded training.

The design is what makes the result decisive. Because the only systematic difference between the full-WIA and the core-and-intensive groups is access to training, the contrast between them isolates the marginal effect of training itself. Two findings stand out:

  1. The cheap, low-intensity human help worked. Intensive (staff-assisted) services raised quarterly earnings by roughly $600, about 17 percent, in the fifth quarter after enrollment [McConnell2016]. Job-search assistance and counseling — not training — delivered a measurable return.
  2. The training did not. At the 30-month follow-up, “the evidence suggests that training funded by the Adult and Dislocated Worker programs does not have positive impacts” on earnings or employment [Fortson2017]. Mathematica’s own summary characterized the training evidence as inconclusive — a careful way of saying that a very large, well-powered RCT could not detect a benefit from the central, most expensive component of the program.

This pattern — counseling helps, generic training doesn’t — recurs across the older evaluation literature. The National JTPA Study (the Job Training Partnership Act, WIA’s predecessor) found that participants “did not see a statistically significant improvement in employment rates, earnings, or continuous employment” [Jacobs2025, summarizing the JTPA evaluation]. And, as noted, TAA training — explicitly designed for trade-displaced workers — fared no better. The common thread is detachment: training delivered through vouchers and individual training accounts, in classroom settings, chosen by the worker from an approved provider list, with no built-in link to a specific employer’s demonstrated demand or to an actual job at the end. The training is supply- driven (whatever providers offer and workers pick) rather than demand-driven (whatever specific employers in a specific sector will actually hire for).

It is worth being precise about what this evidence does and does not show. It does not show that education or skills are worthless — human capital is among the best-documented drivers of lifetime earnings. It shows that the particular institutional form of publicly funded, generic, detached-from-employment retraining, as actually implemented at scale in the United States, has a weak-to-null average return. That is a claim about program design, not about the value of skills. §4 revisits whether the null is partly an artifact of short evaluation windows; even granting that, the design lesson survives.

§3 — What works: sectoral and employer-linked, job-attached programs

Against that backdrop, the results from sectoral training programs are striking. The definitive synthesis is Katz, Roth, Hendra, and Schaberg, “Why Do Sectoral Employment Programs Work? Lessons from WorkAdvance” [Katz2022] (NBER w28248, 2020; Journal of Labor Economics, 2022). The authors pool the evidence from four RCT-based major evaluations — the Sectoral Employment Impact Study (SEIS), the WorkAdvance demonstration, Project QUEST, and Year Up — covering eight programs and providers in total. Their headline finding:

“The sector-focused programs evaluated in these four RCTs generate substantial earnings gains from 14 to 39 percent the year or so following training completion.” [Katz2022]

(The paper’s abstract states the wider envelope across all program-years as 11 to 40 percent; the 14–39 percent figure is the cleaner post-completion synthesis.) The per-program Year-2 gains illustrate the spread:

Program (RCT) Year-2 earnings gain Notes
WorkAdvance (pooled, 4 providers) ~14% NYC, Tulsa, NE Ohio [Katz2022]
SEIS (pooled, 3 programs) ~29% Per Scholas, JVS-Boston, WRTP [Katz2022]
Per Scholas (SEIS-era) ~35% IT training; strongest single provider [Katz2022]
Year Up ~39% youth/young adults [Katz2022]

Three features of this body of evidence matter for design.

(a) The gains are large and they persist. The recurring disappointment of the older active-labor-market literature is fade-out: early gains erode toward zero. The sectoral programs break that pattern. As Katz et al. summarize, “all three evaluations with available longer-term follow-ups (WorkAdvance for six years after random assignment, Project Quest for nine years, and Year Up for three years) show substantial persistence of the early earnings gains with little evidence of the fade out of treatment impacts found in many evaluations of past employment programs” [Katz2022]. The individual programs bear this out:

  • WorkAdvance (MDRC). The two-year evaluation found an average earnings gain of about $1,945 per participant (roughly 14 percent, ~$2,000/year), with Per Scholas the strongest site [Hendra2016]. By the seven-year follow-up, Per Scholas had raised average earnings by about 14 percent in Year 7 and increased the share of participants earning $40,000 or more; three of the four sites raised the share of high earners even where average earnings effects were not significant [Kanengiser2022]. Notably, employment rates were not significantly raised in Year 7 at any site — the gains came from advancement into higher-wage jobs, exactly as the model intended.
  • Project QUEST (San Antonio). A long, college-based healthcare-sector training program with intensive navigation support. The RCT (n=410; 207 treatment, 203 control) found significantly higher annual earnings in years 4–6 and a year-9 gain of roughly $5,000–$6,000, about 15–20 percent, with effects significant or near-significant through years 9–12 [Roder2019]. This is the strongest durability result in the entire literature; the report’s title, “Nine Year Gains,” is the point. Participants’ earnings grew from ~$11,722 to ~$33,644 over the study window.
  • Year Up. A six-month full-time training (IT and financial services) followed by a six-month paid internship at a partner firm, plus a weekly stipend and wraparound supports. The RCT (n≈2,544, eight urban sites, ~85% Black or Hispanic young adults; run by Abt Associates under the federal PACE project) found Year Up raised average quarterly earnings by more than 50 percent in quarters 6–7 — “the largest earnings impacts recorded to date for a rigorously evaluated workforce development program” — and roughly a 40 percent (~$7,011) increase in annual earnings by year 3 [Fein2018]. Longer follow-ups report gains sustained at 30–40 percent ($7,000–$8,000/year) through five and even seven years, accumulating to more than $38,000 in extra earnings by year 7 [Fein2021]. [Note: the “53%” figure sometimes cited appears to conflate the >50% quarterly result with annual figures; the well-anchored numbers are ~40% annual / >50% quarterly early on.]

(b) The mechanism is wage gains via sector access, not just more employment. Katz et al. find that “earnings gains are generated by getting participants into higher-wage jobs in higher-earning industries and occupations not just by raising employment” [Katz2022]. In WorkAdvance, the share of participants earning over $15/hour in Year 2 rose 5.5 percentage points pooled (20.8% → 26.3%), and 16.2 points at Per Scholas alone; higher-wage-employment effects persisted through Year 6 [Katz2022]. The programs move people into a better sector, not merely back into any job.

(c) The active ingredients are identifiable — and they are about employer linkage. Katz et al. attribute the impacts to: training in transferable and certifiable skills (which the authors argue are otherwise under-provided by employers because of poaching concerns — a market failure the programs correct); reductions in barriers to entry into high-wage sectors for non-traditional workers; up-front screening; and wraparound services. To this the program descriptions add the most consequential common feature: the training is demand-driven and employer-facing. WorkAdvance required providers to be “far more employer-facing than traditional training programs,” continuously tracking multiple employers’ changing skill requirements and hiring practices; Year Up ends in an actual job placement (the internship). The training is organized around what specific employers in a specific sector will actually hire for — and, in Year Up’s case, around a literal job at a real firm.

The contrast with §2 is therefore not “training vs. no training.” Both the WIA Gold Standard and the sectoral programs are “training.” They were evaluated with the same gold-standard RCT methodology. The difference is design: detached, supply-driven, classroom training produced nothing; sector-targeted, employer-linked, job-attached training produced 14–39 percent gains that lasted the better part of a decade.

§4 — Counter-evidence and caveats

The thesis is strong, but several caveats temper how confidently it can be applied to a national-scale program for technology-displaced workers.

1. The “training pays off with a lag” literature complicates the simple “classroom fails” story. The most important methodological caveat is that the average ALMP — including classroom training — looks better when you wait. Card, Kluve, and Weber’s meta-analysis of more than 200 program evaluations finds that average impacts are “close to zero in the short run, but become more positive 2–3 years after completion,” and that classroom and on-the-job training “tend to show better outcomes in the medium-run than the short-run” [CardKluveWeber2018]. Two implications follow. First, some of the apparent null from the WIA evaluation (a 30-month window) may reflect a too-short follow-up rather than a true zero — an honest reading must concede this. Second, however, this lag argument rehabilitates generic training only modestly: the sectoral RCTs deliver gains that are both larger in magnitude and more durable (QUEST at 9 years, Year Up at 5–7, WorkAdvance/Per Scholas at 7) than what the medium-run average for generic training implies. The sectoral model dominates on both axes, so the design lesson stands even after granting the lag.

2. Selection. Sectoral programs typically screen applicants up front (motivation, basic skills, sometimes a sobriety or attendance requirement) and are run by unusually capable provider organizations (Per Scholas, Year Up’s national network). The RCTs correctly estimate the effect for the screened population that enrolled, but that population is not a random draw of displaced workers, and the providers are not a random draw of training organizations. A program serving everyone, run by average providers, may not replicate these effects.

3. External validity and scale. The evaluated programs are relatively small and concentrated in a handful of sectors with genuine, persistent demand — IT, healthcare, financial services. It is an open question whether sector strategies can be scaled to absorb national-scale AI displacement across many occupations. Even within the WorkAdvance demonstration, results were strongly provider- and sector-dependent: Per Scholas (IT) carried the durable seven-year gains, while three of four sites showed no significant average-earnings effect at Year 7 [Kanengiser2022]. This is a warning that the model’s success is not automatic; it depends on choosing sectors with real demand and on execution quality.

4. Cost-effectiveness. These are intensive, expensive programs. Year Up costs roughly $28,000 per participant for the program year [Fein2018]. The benefit-cost case is generally positive because the earnings gains are so large and durable — but the per-head cost constrains how widely such a model can be deployed without enormous appropriations, and it raises the bar for any scaled version to maintain quality.

5. The demand-side limit. The most fundamental caveat for the AI context comes from Brookings. Julian Jacobs argues that retraining faces structural limits as an answer to AI displacement: technological change “can reduce the number of available middle-wage ‘skilled’ positions faster than workers can retrain into them”; many vulnerable workers face participation barriers (no savings, competing life demands, proximity to retirement); and program designers cannot reliably forecast which occupations AI will spare, risking retraining people into also-automatable jobs [Jacobs2025]. Crucially, Jacobs concedes that private sectoral training shows “overwhelmingly positive” results — but cautions that those impacts “may not generalize” and, decisively, that even great training does not by itself increase the number of quality jobs available. Training reslots workers into existing demand; it does not create demand. If displacement outpaces the supply of good openings, retraining alone cannot clear the market.

This last point is not a refutation of the §3 thesis; it is the bridge to the policy design. It says that employer-linked, job-attached training is necessary but not sufficient — and that a serious program must also act on labor demand, not just worker supply.

What this implies for the conceptual motivation section and the policy design

For the conceptual motivation section. This review supplies the empirical backbone for the claim that existing retraining policy is inadequate to an AI dislocation shock — but it does so with an important asymmetry that should be foregrounded. The inadequacy is not that “training doesn’t work.” It is that the dominant institutional form of U.S. retraining — generic, supply-driven, voucher-funded, classroom training detached from any specific employer or job — has a weak-to-null average return in the gold-standard RCT [McConnell2016; Fortson2017], while a demonstrably better model exists and has been proven to work [Katz2022]. The motivation section should therefore frame the problem as a design failure of the legacy system, not as a reason for fatalism about human capital. And it should carry forward the Brookings caveat [Jacobs2025] that even the good model cannot, on its own, conjure the jobs into which displaced workers must move — which is precisely why a supply-side intervention must be paired with a demand-side one.

For the policy design. The single most robust, RCT-grade design lesson in the entire workforce literature is this: tie training to actual employment with a real employer. The programs that worked share a job-attachment property that the programs that failed lacked. Year Up ends in a job (the paid internship); WorkAdvance providers are employer-facing and train to documented employer demand; QUEST trains for specific, hiring healthcare occupations. The programs that failed hand a worker a voucher and a provider list. The design implications for a general “technology adjustment assistance” program follow directly:

  1. Make training employer-linked and job-attached by construction. The program should not fund detached classroom seats. It should route assistance through an actual employer and an actual job — which is exactly the logic of a worker-following employment subsidy: the public money follows the displaced worker into a real job at a real firm, rather than into a voucher for speculative classroom training. This converts the strongest empirical finding into the program’s core mechanism.

  2. Make on-the-job training mandatory. Because the active ingredient is certifiable, transferable, demand-driven skill acquisition delivered in connection with employment — and because employers under-provide such training on their own due to poaching concerns [Katz2022] — the subsidy should carry a mandatory OJT requirement, correcting that market failure while ensuring the training is attached to a genuine job and a genuine employer’s demand.

  3. Target sectors with real, persistent demand, and build in provider/sector quality controls. The provider- and sector-dependence of the results [Kanengiser2022] means a scaled program must not assume the Per Scholas result generalizes automatically; it should steer toward sectors with documented hiring demand and condition continued funding on outcomes.

  4. Pair training with a demand-side instrument, and keep cheap job-search/counseling supports. Because training reslots workers into existing demand but does not create it [Jacobs2025], the employment subsidy’s demand-stimulating function is not an add-on — it is what makes the training investment pay off at scale. And because the one cheap thing that did work in the WIA RCT was staff-assisted job search and counseling [McConnell2016], those low-cost supports should be retained as a complement, not discarded.

In short: the evidence does not say “retraining is hopeless.” It says, with unusual clarity, that the kind of retraining matters enormously, and that the kind which works is sectoral, employer-linked, and attached to a real job. A worker-following employment subsidy with mandatory on-the-job training is the policy instrument that operationalizes that finding — and, paired with a demand-side mechanism, it answers the one limit that even good training cannot.

Motivation + PolicyReview 04

04 — Trade Adjustment Assistance: Precedent and Cautionary Tale

Abstract

Trade Adjustment Assistance (TAA) is the closest institutional ancestor of the program this paper proposes. Created by the Trade Expansion Act of 1962 and rebuilt by the Trade Act of 1974, TAA embodies a distinctive policy logic: when a specific, identifiable structural shock — here, import competition and offshoring — concentrates its costs on a subset of workers while the gains diffuse across the whole economy, the displaced are owed a richer-than-ordinary package of “adjustment assistance” [CRS2022; Kennedy1962]. A technology adjustment program inherits that logic almost verbatim, substituting automation and AI for trade as the shock. TAA is therefore the natural reference point — and, on the evidence, the central cautionary tale. The program’s design rests on two pillars that both proved problematic: (1) narrow, causation-gated eligibility, established through a group petition and a Department of Labor finding that foreign competition “contributed importantly” to the layoff [CRS2022]; and (2) a classroom-training-centric adjustment model, with weekly income support (Trade Readjustment Allowances, TRA) conditioned on training enrollment, supplemented by a small wage-insurance program for older workers (ATAA/RTAA) and a Health Coverage Tax Credit. The flagship federal impact evaluation — the Mathematica / Social Policy Research Associates study led by Peter Schochet [Schochet2012] — found that TAA participants did no better, and on key margins worse, than a matched comparison group: roughly $3,273 lower earnings in the fourth year after the initial unemployment-insurance claim, and an employment gap that began more than 25 percentage points wide and had still not fully closed four years out [Schochet2012; CLEAR_TAA]. A separate non-experimental study estimated that participation was associated with a wage loss about 10 percentage points larger than non-participation [ReynoldsPalatucci2008]. The General Accountability Office had warned a decade earlier that the certification process was slow and burdensome, that training waivers were liberally granted, and — most fundamentally — that “programs cannot solve communities’ long-term problems” [GAO2001]. The program lapsed into a phased termination on July 1, 2022 and, as of this writing, has not been reauthorized [CRS2022]. The design lessons for a technology-adjustment program are sharp and largely negative: do not gate eligibility on proving the cause of a job loss; do not build the program around detached classroom training; and do not expect any adjustment program, however generous, to substitute for demand where local demand has collapsed.

§1 — Framing: TAA as the template for shock-specific adjustment assistance

Most labor-market policy is shock-agnostic. Unemployment insurance pays a laid-off worker the same benefit whether the layoff came from a recession, a plant relocation, or a bad quarter. The Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) system serves “dislocated workers” generically, without asking what dislocated them (see the companion review on WIOA fragmentation). Against that backdrop, TAA is unusual and instructive: it is a shock-specific program, built on the proposition that a particular structural force — trade liberalization — produces a particular class of victims who deserve a particular, more-generous response.

The rationale is a compensation argument as old as the modern trade-policy consensus. Reduced trade barriers deliver diffuse gains (lower prices, more variety, export opportunities) but impose concentrated losses on the workers and communities exposed to import competition [CRS2022]. President Kennedy, proposing the original program in 1962, put the logic plainly: “When considerations of national policy make it desirable to avoid higher tariffs, those injured by that competition should not be required to bear the full brunt of the impact” [Kennedy1962]. TAA was, in effect, the side-payment that made trade liberalization politically sustainable — a way to buy the losers’ acquiescence to a policy that benefited the median consumer. For three decades it was explicitly bundled with trade-opening legislation; TAARA’s 2015 reauthorization rode alongside the renewal of Trade Promotion Authority [CRS2022].

The structural parallel to a technology shock is exact, and it is the reason TAA is the template for the program proposed here. Automation and AI, like trade, promise large aggregate gains (higher productivity, cheaper goods and services) while threatening to concentrate the losses on the specific workers whose tasks are displaced. If one accepts the compensation logic for trade, one is committed — by parity of reasoning — to something like it for technology. The phrase “technology adjustment assistance” is a deliberate echo: it imports TAA’s moral and political architecture (a shock-specific, more-than-ordinary response to concentrated dislocation) while proposing to discard TAA’s operational architecture. That is the analytical work this review does. It establishes, with the evidence, why the architecture must be discarded — and which specific design features are the ones to avoid.

A scope note. This review concerns TAA as an institution and a precedent: its design, its history, and its evaluation record as a whole. The narrower question of whether retraining works — the mechanism at TAA’s core — is treated in the companion review on retraining efficacy, which reaches a compatible conclusion: detached, classroom, voucher-style training shows weak-to-null average returns, while sectoral, employer-linked, job-attached training works. TAA is the largest real-world instance of the kind that does not work.

§2 — The design of TAA

Eligibility: the petition and the “contributed importantly” finding

TAA’s defining design feature — and its first trap — is that eligibility is group-based and causation-gated. A worker cannot simply apply. A group of workers (or a union, firm, or state workforce agency acting on their behalf) must file a petition with the Department of Labor (DOL). DOL then conducts an investigation to determine whether “foreign competition ‘contributed importantly’ to the workers’ job loss” and whether the layoff met the statutory criteria of Section 222 of the Trade Act [CRS2022]. Only if DOL certifies the petitioning group do its individual members become eligible to pursue benefits through their state workforce and unemployment-insurance systems [CRS2022].

This architecture has two consequences that recur throughout the evaluation literature. First, it makes eligibility a matter of proving the cause of a layoff — a determination that is contestable, evidence-intensive, and slow. Second, it builds in a structural delay between job loss and assistance: the certification investigation has to run before any benefit can flow. The GAO found these investigations routinely blew through their statutory deadlines (see §4).

Benefits: income support, training, wage insurance, health coverage

Once a group is certified, individual workers may draw on a benefit menu substantially richer than what an ordinary dislocated worker receives [CRS2022]:

  • Training and reemployment services. Subsidized occupational training is the program’s conceptual core, supplemented by case management, job-search assistance, and — for those moving outside their commuting area — job-search and relocation allowances.

  • Trade Readjustment Allowances (TRA). This is TAA’s signature income-support benefit: a weekly payment, equal to the worker’s final weekly unemployment benefit, available after the worker has exhausted regular unemployment insurance (UI) and is enrolled in an eligible training program [CRS2022]. The combined UI-plus-TRA entitlement runs to a maximum of 130 weeks (with the final 13 weeks available only when needed to finish a training program) [CRS2022]. The training-enrollment condition is the load-bearing design choice: TRA channels recipients into classroom training by making extended income support contingent on it.

  • Reemployment Trade Adjustment Assistance (RTAA), the wage-insurance component. For certified workers age 50 and over who take a new job at a lower wage, RTAA pays 50% of the difference between the old and new wage, up to a two-year maximum of $10,000 [CRS2022]. (Under the pre-2021 “Alternative TAA”/ATAA branding the design was substantially the same; later versions raised the cap.) This is the one part of TAA built on a reemployment-first rather than a train-first logic — it pays workers to take a job now and top up their wages, rather than to sit in training. It is also, tellingly, the part with the strongest conceptual claim and the smallest take-up (see below).

  • Health Coverage Tax Credit (HCTC). A credit equal to 72.5% of qualified health-insurance premiums, administered through the tax code and aligned with TRA eligibility; it carried its own sunset and lapsed January 1, 2022 [CRS2022].

A striking feature of the actual benefit take-up exposes the gap between TAA’s design and its practice. In FY2021, 95% of participants received employment services and case management, but only 51% received training — the program’s supposed core — while just 11% collected RTAA wage insurance and less than 1% used a relocation allowance [CRS2022]. Roughly half of all participants in a training-centric program did not train; the most reemployment-oriented benefit reached barely one in ten. The program’s behavioral center of gravity was never where its design placed it.

History: expansion, contraction, and lapse

TAA’s statutory history is a six-decade cycle of expansion (usually bundled with trade-opening) and contraction. The milestones [CRS2022; CRS2015; Kennedy1962]:

  • 1962 — Trade Expansion Act. TAA is created as the side-payment for Kennedy-round liberalization. It offers income support, encourages retraining, and provides relocation allowances — but eligibility requires a U.S. Tariff Commission injury finding, and the process is so burdensome that the program is barely used for its first dozen years [CRS2015; GuthLee_history].

  • 1974 — Trade Act of 1974. The program is rebuilt and liberalized: the injury requirement is dropped, administration moves from the Tariff Commission to DOL, eligibility is loosened, income support is raised, and explicit funding for retraining and job search is added [CRS2015]. Title II of this Act is TAA’s enduring statutory home (19 U.S.C. §2271 et seq.) [CRS2022].

  • 1981. Congress sharply curtails the program at the Reagan administration’s request.

  • 1993–2002. A separate NAFTA-TAA program is created for workers displaced by the North American Free Trade Agreement; the 2002 Trade Act amendments consolidate it back into TAA and add the wage-insurance program (ATAA) and the HCTC [CRS2015]. The 2002-amendment version of the program is the one the flagship federal evaluation studied [Schochet2012].

  • 2009 — ARRA / Trade and Globalization Adjustment Assistance Act. The American Recovery and Reinvestment Act sharply expands TAA, most notably extending eligibility to service-sector workers (not just manufacturing) and raising benefit levels [CRS2015]. This is the program’s high-water mark.

  • 2011 and 2015 — TAAEA and TAARA. Successive reauthorizations restore most of the expanded benefits and extend authorization, the last being the Trade Adjustment Assistance Reauthorization Act of 2015 (TAARA, Title IV of P.L. 114-27), passed alongside the renewal of Trade Promotion Authority [CRS2022].

  • 2021 — “Reversion 2021.” TAARA’s own sunset provisions take effect July 1, 2021, cutting the program back: service workers are dropped (production workers only), “shift in production” eligibility narrows largely to free-trade-agreement partners, and the training-and-activities funding cap falls from $450 million to $220 million [CRS2022].

  • July 1, 2022 — phased termination. Under TAARA’s termination provisions (Section 285 of the Trade Act), DOL may no longer certify new petitions or accept new ones; previously certified workers continue to receive benefits, but the program is otherwise wound down [CRS2022]. The FY2022 pre-sequestration appropriation was $540 million [CRS2022]. Several House-passed vehicles in the 117th Congress (a standalone Modernization Act, Build Back Better, the America COMPETES Act) would have extended TAA through FY2028, but none was enacted [CRS2022].

The bottom line on status: TAA has lapsed. As of this writing it certifies no new workers and awaits a reauthorization that Congress has repeatedly declined to pass. A program that ran for sixty years as the flagship federal response to a specific structural shock has been allowed to die — a fact that is itself part of the cautionary tale, and one a technology-adjustment program should not wish to repeat.

§3 — The evaluation record

TAA is one of the better-evaluated workforce programs in the federal portfolio, and the verdict is consistently disappointing.

The flagship federal evaluation: Schochet et al. (Mathematica / SPR, 2012)

DOL’s Employment and Training Administration commissioned a multi-year, multi-report evaluation of TAA under the 2002 amendments, conducted by Mathematica Policy Research with Social Policy Research Associates. The synthesis report — The Evaluation of the Trade Adjustment Assistance Program: A Synthesis of Major Findings (August 2012) — was authored by Peter Z. Schochet, Ronald D’Amico, Jillian Berk, Sarah Dolfin, and Nathan Wozny [Schochet2012].

The design was a non-experimental, propensity-score-matched comparison. The team matched 2,054 TAA participants drawn from 26 randomly selected states to 1,796 UI claimants in the same local areas who had not been laid off from TAA-certified firms but were otherwise similar, then tracked education, training, employment, earnings, and benefit receipt over roughly four years using surveys and administrative records [CLEAR_TAA; Schochet2012]. (The CLEAR clearinghouse rates the causal evidence “moderate” — a well-implemented non-experimental design, short of an RCT [CLEAR_TAA].)

The findings:

  1. TAA participants did no better — and on earnings, worse — than the comparison group. Over the four-year window, the program had “largely neutral effects on labor-force participation, employment, and earnings,” with the negative earnings result the most quoted: in the fourth year after the initial UI claim, TAA participants earned about $3,273 less than comparison workers [CLEAR_TAA; Schochet2012].

  2. The employment gap opened enormous and closed slowly. Because TAA channeled recipients into long classroom training, participants were out of the labor force longer: their employment rate ran more than 25 percentage points below the comparison group in year one, narrowing to under 10 points by year four — i.e., still not closed at the end of the observation window [CLEAR_TAA]. The training kept people out of work during the years it was supposed to be making them more employable.

  3. TAA succeeded only at producing program activity, not outcomes. Participation sharply increased the receipt of reemployment services and of education and training — exactly what the program was designed to deliver — but this “did not translate into improved earnings outcomes” [Schochet2012]. The program reliably bought services; it did not buy results.

The evaluators offered two main exculpatory hypotheses, which honesty requires carrying forward as caveats (see §4): the four-year window might be too short to capture the delayed payoff of people who spent years in training, and the cohort happened to enter the labor market into the 2008–09 recession, a uniquely hostile reemployment environment [Schochet2012].

Corroborating evidence: Reynolds & Palatucci (2008)

An independent academic study reached a compatible — and on wages, starker — conclusion. Examining manufacturing-sector displacement in 2003–2005, Reynolds and Palatucci estimated that participating in TAA was associated with a wage loss roughly 10 percentage points larger than not participating [ReynoldsPalatucci2008]. The study did find a reemployment-rate advantage for TAA trainees (about 83.9% vs. 73.7%, a ~10-point gap), but the workers who went through TAA training ended up in lower-paying jobs than comparable non-participants [ReynoldsPalatucci2008]. The pattern is the same paradox the Mathematica study found from the other direction: TAA got some people back to work, but into worse jobs, and the wage penalty swamped any employment gain. (This is a non-experimental estimate and shares the selection caveats of all such designs — see §4.)

The take-up evidence on wage insurance

A quieter but important strand concerns the wage-insurance component (ATAA/RTAA), the one part of TAA built on reemployment-first logic. The GAO found that 5% or fewer of participants used wage insurance in FY2006, partly because the 26-week reemployment-eligibility window was too restrictive [GAO_wageins]; FY2021 take-up was still only 11% [CRS2022]. The most conceptually defensible piece of TAA — pay people to take a job and top up the wage, rather than pay them to sit in training — was the least used, in part because the rest of the program’s incentives pulled in the opposite direction.

The consistency across methods, decades, and authors is what makes the record damning. A federally-commissioned matched-comparison study, an independent academic study, and GAO program audits all point the same way: TAA did not raise the earnings of the workers it served, and plausibly lowered them.

§4 — Diagnosis & caveats

Why TAA underperformed

The evaluations tell us TAA did not work; the program’s design tells us why. Three mechanisms stand out, and each maps directly onto a design choice a technology-adjustment program could avoid.

1. Causation-gated eligibility made the program slow, narrow, and adversarial. Because benefits flowed only after DOL certified that foreign competition “contributed importantly” to a layoff [CRS2022], the program built an evidentiary contest between worker and government into the front door. The GAO found the resulting investigations chronically late: 58% of NAFTA-TAA investigations exceeded their 40-day statutory deadline, and 34% of TAA investigations exceeded their 60-day limit [GAO2001]. A worker laid off today might wait months for a certification decision before any TAA-specific benefit could begin — precisely the window in which job search is most effective. Worse, the causation gate is arbitrary at the margin: two workers laid off from the same town in the same month, one from an import-competing plant and one not, get categorically different treatment, even though their reemployment problem is identical. Causation-gating spends administrative effort policing a distinction that does not track need.

2. The classroom-training core was the wrong instrument. TAA’s design pushed recipients into extended classroom training by conditioning its signature income support (TRA) on training enrollment. The Mathematica evaluation shows the predictable result: participants spent years out of the labor force, posted a 25-point early employment deficit, and emerged with lower earnings than comparison workers [Schochet2012]. This is the same detached, supply-driven, voucher-style training model that the gold-standard WIA RCT found produced no earnings gains (see the companion retraining review). The training was not tied to a specific employer’s demonstrated demand or to an actual job at the end; it was generic occupational training chosen from an approved-provider list, delivered in a classroom, disconnected from the labor market it was meant to reconnect workers to. The GAO also documented liberal use of training waivers — many recipients skipped training altogether (in FY2021, only 51% of participants trained at all [CRS2022]) — so the program neither delivered effective training to those who took it nor enforced training on those who were nominally required to take it. It got the worst of both worlds: a training-centric architecture that produced poor training outcomes and widespread non-participation in the central activity.

3. Adjustment assistance cannot conjure local demand. The GAO’s deepest critique is in its 2001 title: Trade Adjustment Assistance: Improvements Necessary, but Programs Cannot Solve Communities’ Long-Term Problems [GAO2001]. The point is structural. TAA’s caseload was concentrated in economically undiversified communities and skewed toward lower-skilled workers (the GAO noted that ~80% of beneficiaries had a high-school education or less, vs. ~42% nationally), where the problem was not that individual workers lacked a credential but that the local economy had lost its employment base [GAO2001]. No amount of income support or retraining puts a worker into a job that does not exist in commuting distance. This is the demand-side limit that recurs throughout this project’s reviews: a supply-side program (fix the worker) is structurally unable to solve a demand-side problem (the jobs are gone). TAA is the historical proof of concept for that limit.

Caveats: how much weight the negative findings can bear

Intellectual honesty requires tempering the verdict in several ways.

  • The flagship study is non-experimental. Schochet et al. used propensity-score matching, not random assignment; CLEAR rates the evidence “moderate,” not “high” [CLEAR_TAA]. Selection on unobservables is a real threat — TAA-certified workers came from import-competing plants and may differ systematically from the matched UI claimants in ways the matching could not capture. The same caveat applies, more strongly, to Reynolds & Palatucci [ReynoldsPalatucci2008]. None of the TAA evidence is RCT-grade; it is suggestive convergence, not proof.

  • The evaluators’ own exculpations may have force. The recession-timing problem is genuine: the studied cohort reentered the labor market in 2008–09, and a too-short four-year window may have truncated the delayed payoff of multi-year training [Schochet2012]. The active-labor-market meta-analytic literature does find that training effects are larger in the medium run than the short run (see the retraining review). A longer follow-up might have looked less bad.

  • But the exculpations rehabilitate TAA only modestly. Even granting a delayed-payoff story, the comparison group faced the same recession, so timing cannot explain a relative deficit; and the sectoral-training literature shows that well-designed training delivers large, durable gains that show up clearly within similar windows. The right reading is not “TAA’s training would have worked given time” but “TAA’s kind of training does not work well regardless.” The design lesson survives the caveats.

  • TAA did some things. It delivered real income support to workers in genuine distress, and its wage-insurance component, though tiny, embodies a sound idea. The critique is not that TAA was cruel or pointless; it is that, judged against its stated goal of restoring participants’ earnings, it failed — and failed in ways traceable to specific, avoidable design choices.

What this implies for the conceptual motivation and policy design

For the conceptual motivation. TAA is the empirical centerpiece of the claim that shock-specific adjustment assistance, as historically built, does not work. It is not a strawman: it is a real, sixty-year, federally-evaluated program built on exactly the compensation logic a technology program would invoke — and it produced no earnings gains, plausibly earnings losses, for the workers it served [Schochet2012; ReynoldsPalatucci2008]. The motivation section should use TAA to make two moves at once. First, it validates the premise of a technology-adjustment program: the concentrated-loss-from-diffuse-gain structure of an AI shock is the same structure that has long justified TAA, so the case for some shock-specific response is strong. Second, it forecloses the obvious design: simply rebuilding TAA for technology — petition-and-certify eligibility plus funded classroom retraining — would reproduce a documented failure. The program must keep TAA’s purpose and discard TAA’s machinery. That the actual TAA was allowed to lapse into termination in 2022 [CRS2022], unmourned and unreplaced, only underscores that the legacy model commands neither empirical support nor political durability.

For the policy design. TAA converts directly into a list of design traps to avoid, each paired with a positive alternative:

  1. Avoid causation-gated eligibility. TAA’s petition-and-“contributed importantly”-finding gate was slow (statutory deadlines routinely missed [GAO2001]), arbitrary at the margin, and adversarial. A technology-adjustment program should not require a worker (or DOL) to prove that AI specifically caused a given layoff — an even harder causal attribution than trade, since automation’s fingerprints are diffuse and contested. Eligibility should key on the fact and circumstances of dislocation and on demonstrable local labor-market conditions, not on litigating the cause. This both speeds assistance to where it is needed and avoids spending administrative resources policing a distinction that does not track need.

  2. Avoid detached classroom training as the core. TAA’s train-first architecture kept workers out of the labor market for years and left them with lower earnings [Schochet2012]. The retraining review establishes the alternative the evidence supports: training that is employer-linked and attached to a real job. A technology-adjustment program should make its core mechanism a worker-following employment subsidy — public money that flows into an actual job at an actual firm, with any training delivered on the job — rather than a TRA-style income stream conditioned on sitting in a classroom. Reemployment-first, not train-first.

  3. Target demand, and tie assistance to real jobs. TAA’s deepest failure was structural: it could not put workers into jobs that did not exist locally [GAO2001]. A serious technology program must therefore act on labor demand — subsidizing actual hiring and tying every dollar of assistance to a real, filled job — rather than only improving worker supply and hoping demand materializes. The single most reemployment-oriented piece of TAA, the wage-insurance component, pointed in this direction but was starved of take-up (≤5–11% [GAO_wageins; CRS2022]) by a program whose incentives pulled the other way. The technology program should make the wage-insurance/ employment-subsidy logic its spine, not its afterthought.

In short: TAA establishes both the legitimacy of a shock-specific adjustment program and the specific design failures such a program must avoid. The technology-adjustment program proposed here is best understood as TAA’s purpose executed on the opposite design principles — eligibility that does not require proving causation, assistance that follows the worker into a real job rather than into a classroom, and a demand-side instrument that funds hiring rather than hoping for it.

05 — Government Job-Guarantee Proposals: The Political Alternative

Abstract

As anxiety about AI-driven labor displacement intensifies, the most politically salient left-of-center response is the job guarantee (JG): a public commitment that government will, as employer of last resort, supply a job to anyone who wants one at a fixed living wage. This review surveys the JG’s academic foundation, its live political proposals (2018–2026), and the standard critiques, in order to situate it against the market-oriented technology adjustment assistance program this paper proposes. The intellectual core comes from Pavlina Tcherneva and the Levy Economics Institute’s Modern Monetary Theory (MMT) tradition, which reframes the JG as an automatic macroeconomic stabilizer — a “buffer stock of employed people” replacing the conventional “buffer stock of unemployed people” [Tcherneva2018]. Politically, the idea has migrated from Bernie Sanders’s 2018 federal proposal [Sanders2018] into the AI-displacement debate, most directly in Tom Steyer’s May 2026 California gubernatorial plan, “A Jobs Guarantee for the AI Era” [Steyer2026], and, more cautiously, in Governor Gavin Newsom’s May 2026 executive order directing state agencies to study AI workforce disruption [NewsomEO2026]. The standard critiques — inflation/wage-floor distortion, fiscal cost, “make-work”/low productivity, administrative capacity, crowd-out of private employment, and the political difficulty of winding the program down — are documented here, distinguishing neutral evidence from advocacy. The upshot for our conceptual-motivation section: there is genuine and growing political demand for a guarantee against displacement, but the public-make-work design is the weakest-evidenced way to satisfy it. A program that guarantees access to subsidized private employment can capture the appeal of a “guarantee” while avoiding the JG’s characteristic pathologies.

§1 — Framing: why the job guarantee is the AI-anxiety answer on the left

The political economy of automation anxiety creates demand for a guarantee. When workers fear that a general-purpose technology will erase their occupation, incremental labor-market tools — a training voucher here, a wage-insurance pilot there — read as inadequate to the scale of the threat. The job guarantee answers that fear with a single, legible promise: if AI takes your job, the government will give you one. Its rhetorical power is precisely its simplicity, and that simplicity is why it dominates the left-of-center conversation about AI and work.

Three forces have pushed the JG up the agenda. First, the headline projections. Senator Bernie Sanders’s HELP Committee released a 2025 staff report warning that AI and automation “could eliminate nearly 100 million U.S. jobs” over roughly a decade, with illustrative estimates such as 89 percent of fast-food and counter workers, 64 percent of accountants, and 47 percent of truck drivers displaced [Sanders2025]. The report’s methodology is itself notable — staff derived task-automation shares by querying a large language model about more than 700 BLS occupational profiles — and the report explicitly hedges that the figure represents “one potential future” rather than a forecast [Sanders2025]. (We flag this as a motivated, non-neutral estimate; see Review 01 for the empirical reallocation literature.) Whatever its rigor, the “100 million jobs” number has become the rhetorical anchor of the displacement debate.

Second, visible tech-sector layoffs. A wave of high-profile reductions in which employers cited AI as a contributing factor — reported alongside layoffs at large technology firms — converted the abstraction of automation into salient news, and put direct political pressure on officials, especially in California [NewsomEO2026; Steyer2026].

Third, an intellectual vehicle ready to receive the anxiety. The MMT-adjacent job-guarantee literature, developed over two decades at the Levy Economics Institute, supplied a fully worked-out program design just as the political demand crested [Tcherneva2018]. The JG was not invented for the AI moment; it was repurposed for it.

It is worth distinguishing the JG from its frequent companion on the left, universal basic income (UBI). UBI offers income without work; the JG offers work (with income attached). JG advocates argue this is a feature — that paid work confers dignity, social connection, and skill maintenance that a cash transfer does not — and explicitly position the JG as superior to UBI on those grounds [Tcherneva2018]. For our purposes the relevant point is that the JG, unlike UBI, is a labor-market intervention and therefore the direct competitor to any labor-market program we propose.

§2 — The academic foundation: employer of last resort and the MMT framing

The modern JG’s intellectual core is the employer-of-last-resort (ELR) proposal developed at the Levy Economics Institute, of which Pavlina Tcherneva’s Working Paper No. 902, The Job Guarantee: Design, Jobs, and Implementation (2018), is the canonical operational blueprint [Tcherneva2018]. (The lineage runs back through Levy work such as Wray and Forstater’s ELR papers; WP 902 is the consolidated design document.)

Tcherneva defines the JG as “a permanent, federally funded, and locally administered program that supplies voluntary employment opportunities on demand for all who are ready and willing to work at a living wage” [Tcherneva2018]. Four design commitments follow:

  1. A fixed, uniform wage. WP 902 specifies “$15 per hour plus benefits” as the wage-benefit package [Tcherneva2018]. Crucially, the wage is not meant to clear a market; it is meant to set a floor. By guaranteeing a public job at $15+benefits, the JG “establishes the labor standard that must be met by all employers” — any private employer offering worse pay or conditions would, in theory, have to match the JG to retain workers [Tcherneva2018].

  2. Federally funded, locally administered. Funding comes from the federal government (for “financial sustainability reasons” grounded in MMT’s view of currency-issuer fiscal space), while administration runs through “local and municipal governments, nonprofits and/or social enterprises, and cooperatives,” which design and run the actual projects [Tcherneva2018].

  3. Voluntary and “on demand.” The program is permanent and open-ended: anyone of legal working age can take a JG job at any point in the business cycle. It is framed as a “public option for jobs” and an “add-on” that does not displace existing programs; workers can choose between unemployment insurance and the JG [Tcherneva2018].

  4. The buffer-stock-of-employment mechanism. This is the analytically distinctive claim. Conventional macro policy, in the MMT reading, stabilizes prices by maintaining a “reserve army of the unemployed” — a buffer stock of jobless people whose numbers rise and fall to discipline wages (the NAIRU). The JG inverts this: it maintains a buffer stock of employed people. The pool “expands and contracts with recessions and expansions,” automatically countercyclical, “never allowing individuals and the economy to suffer the consequences of unemployment” [Tcherneva2018]. Tcherneva further argues the fixed JG wage is anti-inflationary, because it anchors the price of labor rather than bidding it up: the JG “enhance[s] price stability by using the JG’s buffer stock mechanism and minimum wage feature” [Tcherneva2018].

On scale and cost, WP 902 estimates that a JG launched into today’s labor market “might grow to 16 million workers,” settling to a long-run pool of “anywhere between 2 percent and 10 percent of the labor force” as it improves private-sector conditions [Tcherneva2018]. It cites Fullwiler’s simulations finding the JG would raise real GDP by “$313–560 billion per year” and “permanently add up to four million net new private sector jobs,” and points to Argentina’s Plan Jefes (which employed ~13 percent of the labor force and was modeled on the Levy ELR design) as proof of feasibility at scale [Tcherneva2018].

The most recent Levy work extends the JG into climate and disaster terrain. Tcherneva’s Working Paper No. 1112, After the Wreckage: The Job Guarantee as a New Labor Standard (April 2026), and the related “Green Job Guarantee” stream tie the program to disaster-response and climate-resilience work, arguing that society “will pay” either reactively (disaster relief, lost productivity) or proactively (a JG that builds resilience and “good jobs”) [Tcherneva2026]. This matters for the AI debate because it shows the JG framework being repositioned as the all-purpose answer to any large shock — climate, disaster, and now AI displacement.

A methodological note on the foundation: the JG literature is programmatic advocacy produced by its own proponents, not independent program evaluation. Its central empirical claims — the buffer-stock disinflation mechanism, the four-million private-jobs spillover — rest on simulations and on the Argentine Jefes episode, not on randomized or quasi-experimental evaluation in a developed-economy context. We treat WP 902 as the authoritative statement of the design and the case for the JG, not as neutral evidence that it works.

§3 — Live proposals (2018–2026)

Sanders’s federal job guarantee (2018)

The contemporary political wave begins with Senator Bernie Sanders’s April 2018 proposal for the federal government to “guarantee a job paying $15 an hour and health-care benefits to every American worker ‘who wants or needs one’” [Sanders2018]. Jobs would be in infrastructure, education, the environment, and caregiving, structured as federally funded public works at the $15 wage with benefits including paid family and sick leave. Reporting at the time noted that Sanders’s office “had not done a cost estimate” or identified a funding source [Sanders2018; WaPo2018]. The 2018 proposal is the direct political ancestor of every later JG plan, and its design (federal money, $15 wage, public-purpose jobs) tracks the Levy blueprint closely. Sanders’s 2025 “100 million jobs” report [Sanders2025] then reattached that template to the AI threat, pairing it with a 32-hour workweek, a $17 federal minimum wage, and worker equity stakes in automated industries.

Steyer’s “A Jobs Guarantee for the AI Era” (2026) — the freshest, most direct parallel

The single most directly parallel proposal is former hedge-fund manager and climate philanthropist Tom Steyer’s “A Jobs Guarantee for the AI Era,” unveiled on May 8, 2026 as part of his California gubernatorial campaign [Steyer2026]. (Confirmed via tomsteyer.com, Common Dreams, and The Nation.) It is notable as the first attempt by a serious candidate for major executive office to build a campaign around an AI-specific job guarantee. The plan has several interlocking mechanisms:

  • A “token tax.” Steyer proposes a ballot measure for a tax on corporate AI use — “a fraction of a cent for every unit of data processed by large AI companies” [Steyer2026]. The exact rate is not specified in the campaign materials. [VERIFY: no published per-token rate or revenue estimate located.]

  • A “Golden State Sovereign Wealth Fund.” Token-tax revenue would capitalize a state sovereign-wealth fund investing in “infrastructure, clean energy, housing, education, healthcare, and public services,” with The Nation’s reporting also describing “cash dividends” and investments in “education, training, and job opportunities” [Steyer2026].

  • A jobs guarantee for AI-displaced workers. A “California Jobs Initiative” promising “hundreds of thousands of good-paying jobs that AI cannot replace” — grid upgrades, affordable housing, clean energy, wildfire prevention, healthcare, education, parks — explicitly aimed at guaranteeing employment to workers displaced by AI [Steyer2026]. No specific wage figure or guaranteed job count is published. [VERIFY: no per-worker wage or enrollment target located.]

  • An “AI Worker Protection Administration (AIWPA).” A new state agency dedicated to protecting workers from AI displacement, enforcing workplace protections, supporting job creation, and convening labor, technologists, academics, and civil society to set “worker-centered AI standards” [Steyer2026].

  • Expanded UI and apprenticeship. The plan expands unemployment insurance and “dramatically expanded apprenticeship and workforce training programs statewide” [Steyer2026].

Steyer’s framing is explicitly populist and anti–Big Tech: “the people who stand to profit the most from this technology shouldn’t be making the rules about how it is used,” and “we can’t have 12 trillionaires and 40 million people who can’t make rent” [Steyer2026]. The proposal thus fuses three distinct ideas — a Pigouvian/extractive tax on AI, an Alaska-style sovereign-wealth-fund-cum-dividend, and a Levy-style job guarantee — into one campaign platform. For our paper it is the cleanest example of the guarantee instinct applied directly to the AI shock, and therefore the most important foil.

Newsom’s executive order (2026) — the cautious institutional version

Governor Gavin Newsom signed Executive Order N-6-26 on May 21, 2026, described as a “first-of-its-kind” order to prepare workers and businesses for AI disruption [NewsomEO2026]. (Confirmed via gov.ca.gov, Bloomberg Law, KQED, and Fisher Phillips.) Critically, the EO is a study-and-recommend instrument, not a guarantee: it “does not create any immediate legal obligations for private employers” and instead sets review tasks for state agencies, principally the Labor and Workforce Development Agency (LWDA) and the Employment Development Department (EDD) [NewsomEO2026; FisherPhillips2026]. Reported components include:

  • An AI impact dashboard (EDD), to be launched within ~90 days, tracking AI’s employment effects across sectors using UI data [NewsomEO2026].
  • A California WARN Act review (LWDA), with recommendations within 180 days on revising the state plant-closing/layoff-notice law to provide early-warning data on emerging trends [NewsomEO2026; FisherPhillips2026].
  • An “AI playbook” to modernize job-training programs, with attention to white-collar roles (customer service, software, marketing/sales) flagged as exposed [NewsomEO2026].
  • A directive to explore safety-net policies — severance standards, transition support, “worker ownership models,” and notably “universal basic capital concepts” — and a separate October 15, 2026 deadline for the LWDA to review how collective bargaining and training programs are adapting to AI [NewsomEO2026; FisherPhillips2026].

The Newsom EO is significant for what it is not: it stops well short of a job guarantee, opting for data infrastructure, early-warning reform, and an explicit menu that includes capital-based (“universal basic capital,” worker ownership) rather than purely make-work approaches. It thus represents a more market-curious, evidence-gathering posture within the same Democratic coalition that produces the Steyer/Sanders guarantee — useful for our paper as evidence that even sympathetic officials are reaching past public make-work.

Others

The Sanders 2018 design was elaborated by economists at the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities and by Paul, Darity, and Hamilton (the “Federal Job Guarantee” proposal), which Third Way and others cost at roughly $560–840 billion per year for 10–15 million participants [ThirdWay2018]. These academic plans, alongside the Levy blueprint, form the menu from which the 2026 political proposals draw.

§4 — Critiques and the evidence against public-employment programs

The critiques fall into six recurring categories. Throughout, we distinguish advocacy sources (Third Way, center-left; Cato/Heritage, libertarian/conservative) from neutral evidence.

1. Inflation and wage-floor distortion. The most cited concern is that a large public job at a fixed $15+benefits wage functions as a binding economy-wide wage floor. Critics — including the progressive economist Dean Baker — warn of a “wage-price spiral” as private employers are forced to match the JG package, raising business costs across the economy [ThirdWay2018; Cato2018]. JG advocates contest this directly: Tcherneva argues the fixed wage is disinflationary because it anchors rather than bids up the price of labor [Tcherneva2018], and Mitchell and Muysken argue a JG “introduces no relative wage effects” [MitchellMuysken]. This is a genuine theoretical dispute, not a settled empirical finding; we report it as contested.

2. Fiscal cost. Third Way (center-left advocacy) puts the annual cost at $468–702 billion for the Tcherneva design and $560–840 billion for the Paul et al. design (10–15 million participants), warning that if “just half of low-wage private sector workers quit” for JG jobs, cost could reach “$1.6 trillion to $2.24 trillion per year” [ThirdWay2018]. The Levy framing counters that unemployment is “already ‘paid for’” in real and social costs, so the JG redirects existing resources rather than adding net cost [Tcherneva2018]. The headline numbers are large under any accounting.

3. “Make-work” and low productivity. A standard objection is that genuinely useful jobs that can be created on demand, locally, for anyone regardless of skill, will tend toward low marginal product — i.e., “make-work.” This is the crux for our paper: public-employment programs score least well in the active-labor-market-policy (ALMP) evaluation literature on post-program private-employment outcomes (detailed in the sibling retraining/ALMP review, 03), precisely because they often fail to build the human capital or signal that private employers value. Critics frame this as the JG “not address[ing] actual employment barriers like skills gaps, geographic mismatches, [and] childcare” [ThirdWay2018].

4. Administrative capacity. Matching millions of heterogeneous workers to productive projects, defining eligible work, building training and supervision systems, and enforcing performance standards across thousands of local administrators is described even by sympathetic analysts as an “administrative nightmare” [ThirdWay2018]. The Levy design’s reliance on local governments, nonprofits, and cooperatives multiplies the coordination problem.

5. Crowding out private (and existing public) employment. With ~54 million U.S. workers earning $15/hour or less, Third Way estimates that a JG could draw 35–40 million workers out of private jobs, hollowing out the low-wage private labor market [ThirdWay2018]. Empirically, the closest natural experiment is India’s NREGA rural employment guarantee, where studies found some crowd-out of private employment (and, in some studies, reduced school enrollment) [Wikipedia_JG]. [VERIFY: NREGA crowd-out is real in the literature but magnitude/sign is contested across studies; treat as suggestive, not dispositive.]

6. Difficulty winding down. A permanent public-employment program, once it employs millions and becomes a community institution, is politically very hard to shrink — even though the Levy design intends the pool to contract automatically in expansions [Tcherneva2018]. The asymmetry between the program’s automatic-stabilizer ideal and its likely political stickiness is a recurring critique: a buffer stock that only ratchets up is not a stabilizer but a permanent expansion of the public payroll.

A revealing data point on political viability: Third Way cites polling in which only ~9 percent of voters support guaranteed government jobs, versus ~36 percent favoring public-private partnerships to create private-sector jobs [ThirdWay2018]. [VERIFY: poll wording/source within the Third Way memo not independently confirmed.] If accurate, even the public prefers a market-channel guarantee to a public-make-work guarantee — directly relevant to our framing.

The neutral institutional literature is more measured than either advocacy pole. A 2026 Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis review of “policy responses to weak labor demand” treats the job guarantee as one option among several (alongside wage subsidies and demand management), neither endorsing nor dismissing it [Minneapolis2026]. The honest summary is that the JG’s strongest claims rest on theory and on a small set of developing-country programs, while its costs and crowd-out risks are concrete and large.

What this implies for the conceptual motivation section

The job guarantee is the political alternative our paper must answer, not ignore. Three conclusions follow for the conceptual-motivation section:

  1. The demand for a “guarantee” is real and rising. From Sanders (2018) to Steyer’s AI-era plan and Newsom’s EO (both May 2026), the displacement anxiety is converging on the language of a guarantee against being left behind. A market-oriented program that ignores this emotional and political demand will lose the argument by default. Our technology adjustment assistance program should be framed as also a guarantee — a guarantee of continued attachment to work — not as a technocratic alternative to the very idea of a guarantee.

  2. Public make-work is the weakest-evidenced way to deliver it. The JG’s distinctive design — government as direct employer at a fixed wage, on demand, for anyone — concentrates the program’s worst risks: inflationary wage-floor distortion, very large fiscal cost, low-productivity make-work, severe administrative complexity, crowd-out of the private low-wage market, and a one-way ratchet that resists wind-down. The ALMP evaluation evidence (sibling Review 03) ranks public-employment programs least effective at producing durable private-sector employment — the outcome that ultimately matters.

  3. The synthesis: guarantee access to subsidized private employment. The appeal of the JG is the promise that no displaced worker is abandoned; its pathologies all stem from making the government the employer. A program that instead guarantees displaced workers access to subsidized private-sector jobs — pairing them with real employers, with the subsidy phasing out as productivity rises — captures the political appeal of a guarantee while routing it through the market. It avoids the wage-floor distortion (the subsidy adjusts to the market wage rather than setting it), the make-work problem (the work is real private-sector work), the worst of the administrative burden (employers do the job-matching and supervision), and the wind-down trap (subsidies are temporary and tied to displacement events). Even the polling that JG critics cite suggests the public prefers exactly this market-channel design [ThirdWay2018]. Tellingly, Newsom’s own EO is already reaching past public make-work toward capital- and partnership-based ideas [NewsomEO2026].

In short: the working paper should concede the legitimacy of the guarantee impulse, then argue that a market-oriented adjustment program is the better-designed way to honor it — answering the same anxiety that animates Steyer and Sanders, but without building a permanent public make-work bureaucracy.

Motivation + PolicyReview 06

06 — What Works in Active Labor Market Policy: The International Evidence

Abstract

This review synthesizes the international evidence on which categories of active labor market policy (ALMP) actually work, with a view to what that record implies for a general technology adjustment assistance program. The evidence base is unusually deep: the question has been attacked not with one or two studies but with two large, authoritative meta-analyses covering hundreds of microeconometric evaluations across dozens of countries [CardKluveWeber2010; CardKluveWeber2018], supplemented by gold-standard randomized trials of subsidized employment in the United States [MDRC2017] and by OECD syntheses of hiring-subsidy design [Brown2015; OECD2025]. The findings converge on a clear ranking. Job-search assistance and related “work-first” programs (counseling, monitoring, sanctions) are cheap and deliver relatively favorable impacts in the short run. Training programs are weak or even negative in the short run but turn positive in the medium run (roughly two to three years out), because the lock-in cost of being in a classroom is paid up front while the human-capital return accrues later. Subsidized public-sector employment is the consistent laggard — the least favorable category in every cut of the data, frequently zero or negative. Private-sector employment and wage subsidies sit in between: moderately effective, sometimes quite cost-effective when well targeted, but vulnerable to three indirect costs — deadweight, substitution, and displacement — that can erode much of the apparent gain. Two cross-cutting findings sharpen the policy lesson: average impacts grow more positive over longer horizons, and impacts are larger for the long-term unemployed and the disadvantaged — precisely the groups a dislocation shock concentrates harm on. The dominant design implication is that the evidence favors a combination of private-employer wage/employment subsidies plus job-search assistance over government make-work, and that the case for subsidies is conditional on design features — targeting, retention requirements, and demand-contingent triggers — that hold deadweight and substitution in check. The principal caveats — publication bias (which the meta-analyses test for and largely dismiss), external validity across institutional settings, large measured deadweight in some subsidy schemes, and the fact that public employment programs sometimes pursue non-employment goals — are taken up in §4.

§1 — Framing: A taxonomy of ALMPs and what “works” means

“Active” labor market policy is the family of interventions that try to move people into work, as distinct from “passive” policy (unemployment insurance, welfare) that supports people out of work. The companion reviews frame AI’s labor impact as a dislocation and reallocation shock — demand collapses in some occupations and rises in others, with no frictionless transition between them — and ask which instruments can bridge that gap. ALMPs are the standard toolkit, and the international evaluation literature has spent three decades testing them. Before turning to the evidence it is worth fixing the categories, because the central finding of this literature is that the category of program matters more than almost anything else.

The canonical taxonomy, used by Card, Kluve, and Weber and by the OECD, sorts ALMPs into four families [CardKluveWeber2010]:

  1. Job-search assistance (JSA) and related services — counseling, job-matching, monitoring of search effort, and sanctions for failing to search. These are short, cheap, and impose little or no “lock-in” (participants remain available to take a job immediately).
  2. Training — classroom or on-the-job training intended to build human capital. These are longer and more expensive, and impose a real lock-in cost: time in a classroom is time not spent searching or working.
  3. Subsidized private-sector employment — wage or hiring subsidies that pay an employer (or the worker) to create or fill a job in a real firm facing real product-market discipline.
  4. Subsidized public-sector employment — direct job creation in the public or nonprofit sector, often explicitly as “make-work” or “transitional jobs” for the hard-to-employ.

Two definitional points matter for the policy that follows. First, Card, Kluve, and Weber deliberately restricted their subsidy category to individual-level subsidies and excluded “firm-level subsidy programmes that allow employers to select the individuals whose jobs are subsidised” [CardKluveWeber2010]. That exclusion is important: a worker-following subsidy of the kind contemplated for a technology adjustment program is closer to the individual-level design they studied than to a blanket firm-level credit, which bears on how directly their findings transfer. Second, “works” is not a single thing. The literature distinguishes (a) the sign and significance of the impact on participants’ employment or earnings, (b) the time horizon — short run (~1 year after program completion), medium run (~2 years), long run (~3+ years) — and (c) cost-effectiveness, the impact set against the program’s price. A program can “work” on participant outcomes yet fail on cost-benefit grounds if it is expensive, and a program can show favorable participant impacts that vanish at the macro level once the jobs it fills are jobs taken from someone else (displacement). Holding these three meanings distinct is what allows the evidence to discriminate among the four families rather than delivering the unhelpful verdict that “some programs work and some don’t.”

The organizing thesis is the ranking that emerges robustly across the evidence: JSA favorable and cheap, especially short-run; training positive but lagged; private subsidies moderate but caveat-laden; public employment least effective, often zero or negative. §2 establishes that ranking from the meta-analytic evidence; §3 zooms in on the private-subsidy case and its three indirect costs; §4 takes the strongest counter-arguments seriously; and the closing section draws the design implication.

§2 — The meta-analytic evidence: Card, Kluve, and Weber

The authoritative sources are two meta-analyses by David Card, Jochen Kluve, and Andrea Weber. The first, published in the Economic Journal in 2010, codes 199 program impacts from 97 studies conducted between 1995 and 2007, drawn from a comprehensive survey of IZA and NBER labor researchers across 26 countries [CardKluveWeber2010]. The second, “What Works? A Meta Analysis of Recent Active Labor Market Program Evaluations,” published in the Journal of the European Economic Association in 2018, expands the sample to over 200 studies (the authors’ count of distinct program estimates runs higher still) and refines the analysis by program type, participant group, and three post-program time horizons [CardKluveWeber2018]. Both papers are methodologically careful in a way that matters for a policy brief: because the underlying studies use wildly heterogeneous outcomes (earnings, employment probability, time-to-exit from registered unemployment), the authors classify each estimate simply by whether it is significantly positive, insignificant, or significantly negative, and then show — via an ordered-probit model and a parallel effect-size regression on the subset of employment-outcome studies — that this “sign and significance” approach yields the same conclusions as a conventional effect-size meta-analysis [CardKluveWeber2010].

The ranking by program type

The 2010 paper’s headline is stated in its own abstract: “Job search assistance programmes yield relatively favourable programme impacts, whereas public sector employment programmes are less effective. Training programmes are associated with positive medium-term impacts, although in the short term they often appear ineffective” [CardKluveWeber2010]. In the multivariate models, with combination/”other” programs as the omitted base, the short-run ordering is exactly as the thesis predicts: job-search assistance carries a positive coefficient (weakly more successful), training is roughly neutral-to-negative, subsidized private-sector jobs are negative, and subsidized public-sector jobs are the most negative — “the ‘least successful’ programmes,” in the authors’ words [CardKluveWeber2010].

The cleanest quantitative statement comes from the subsample of 79 estimates that use the probability of employment as the outcome, where the authors can read off comparable ordered-probit coefficients (Table 10 of the 2010 paper). Relative to the omitted “mixed/other” group, the short-run coefficients are approximately −0.4 for job-search assistance, −1.0 for classroom/ on-the-job training, −1.5 for subsidized private-sector employment, and −2.5 for subsidized public-sector employment [CardKluveWeber2010]. (All four are negative here because the omitted category is combination programs, which tend to score well; the ordering is the point.) The monotone descent — JSA best, public employment worst, private subsidies above public but below JSA and training — is the ranking the rest of the literature keeps recovering, and it is remarkably stable: the corresponding effect-size regression produces coefficients that are nearly exact linear rescalings of the probit coefficients (correlation 0.93), so the conclusion does not depend on the choice of method [CardKluveWeber2010].

The ranking by time horizon

The second robust finding is temporal. In the overall 2010 sample, 39% of short-run estimates (≈12 months out) are significantly positive against 25% significantly negative; by the medium run (~24 months) the split is 45% positive against only 10% negative; and in the long run (~36+ months) it is 53% positive against 6% negative [CardKluveWeber2010]. Programs become more favorable as the horizon lengthens. The 2018 update states the same pattern more sharply: “average impacts are close to zero in the short run, but become more positive 2–3 years after completion of the program,” with “larger average gains for programs that emphasize human capital accumulation” [CardKluveWeber2018].

Crucially, the time profile differs by program type. Training is the textbook case of a lagged return: it is weak or negative in the short run because of lock-in (time in class is time not working), but its coefficient rises significantly between the short and medium run [CardKluveWeber2010]. Card, Kluve, and Weber note the parallel to a U.S. welfare-reform re-analysis finding that “although job search assistance programmes dominate training in the short run, over longer horizons the gains to human capital development policies are larger” [CardKluveWeber2010]. JSA, by contrast, front-loads its (smaller) gains. This is the empirical basis for the project’s framing that job-search assistance is the cheap short-run win and training is the patient medium-run bet — and for treating them as complements rather than substitutes.

Heterogeneity: who benefits

A third finding bears directly on a dislocation program’s target population. The 2018 analysis documents “systematic heterogeneity across participant groups, with larger impacts for females and participants who enter from long term unemployment,” and finds that ALMPs “are more likely to show positive impacts in a recession” [CardKluveWeber2018]. In other words, the programs work best for the people a technology shock pushes into long-term joblessness, and best when the labor market is slack — exactly the conditions a demand-side adjustment program is built to address. (The 2010 paper found programs for youth-only groups less favorable than untargeted programs and, against some earlier reviews, no systematic gender difference; the stronger long-term-unemployed result is a feature of the larger 2018 sample [CardKluveWeber2010; CardKluveWeber2018].)

Two methodological reassurances round out the picture. First, the meta-analyses find no significant difference between experimental and non-experimental estimates once program type and participant group are controlled, which means the large observational European literature can be read alongside the smaller RCT literature [CardKluveWeber2010]. Second, and addressed further in §4, they test for publication bias and find none — published and unpublished studies are equally likely to report positive effects [CardKluveWeber2010].

§3 — Private wage/employment subsidies specifically

For a program built around a worker-following employment subsidy, the private-subsidy evidence is the load-bearing piece, so it deserves its own treatment. The meta-analytic verdict is “moderate”: private-sector subsidies out-perform public-sector employment in every cut of the Card-Kluve-Weber data, and their medium-run coefficients turn positive, but they trail JSA and medium-run training [CardKluveWeber2010; CardKluveWeber2018]. The OECD’s synthesis work reaches a compatible conclusion — that “many unemployed benefit from early intervention through the provision of counselling and job-search assistance, and others benefit through targeted employment subsidies, particularly in the private sector” — while stressing that subsidies “are not a panacea” and “can have substantial deadweight and displacement costs” [OECD2025]. [speculative — the precise OECD chapter and page for the “particularly in the private sector” formulation should be pinned to a specific Employment Outlook edition before citation; flagged in the bib as VERIFY.]

The reason subsidies are “moderate rather than excellent” is that their gross effect on participants must be discounted by three well-known indirect costs, each of which transfers part of the benefit away from the policy’s intended target [Brown2015; OECD2025]:

  • Deadweight — the subsidy pays for hires that would have happened anyway. Estimates are often large: one synthesis reports deadweight of around 84% for a broad French low-wage subsidy, falling substantially (a 20–30 percentage-point reduction) when eligibility is restricted to the unemployed rather than offered for all hires [Brown2015].
  • Substitution — the subsidized worker is hired instead of an equivalent unsubsidized worker at the same firm, so net employment does not rise; the subsidy merely reshuffles who holds the job.
  • Displacement — the subsidized firm expands at the expense of unsubsidized competitors, so jobs created in one firm are destroyed in another. Measured displacement varies enormously by country and design: roughly 65–70% in Sweden, 50% in the Netherlands, 36% in Belgium, 20% in the UK and Ireland, and essentially none detected in France [Brown2015]. The spread itself is the lesson — displacement is a design and institutional variable, not a fixed tax.

The constructive half of the same literature is that good design demonstrably shrinks these costs. The IZA World of Labor synthesis distills the design levers: target the long-term unemployed specifically (who have low counterfactual hire rates, minimizing deadweight); restrict the subsidy to net new hires; time-limit it; scale it inversely to the worker’s qualifications (bigger subsidy for the hardest to place); monitor to deter displacement; and limit caseworker discretion to curb cream-skimming [Brown2015]. The same source notes that hiring subsidies, which attach to bringing an outsider in, tend to generate lower lock-in and wage-inflation effects than open-ended wage subsidies, precisely because they are temporary and outsider-targeted [Brown2015]. The net assessment is that hiring subsidies “can be cost-effective when appropriately targeted, particularly benefiting long-term unemployed populations” — but that “success strongly depends on design features” [Brown2015]. This is the empirical warrant for the proposed program’s emphasis on targeting, net-new-hire and retention requirements, and demand-contingent triggers.

A note on the contrast with public-sector subsidized employment, which the U.S. RCT evidence makes vivid. The Enhanced Transitional Jobs Demonstration (ETJD, U.S. Department of Labor) and the parallel Subsidized and Transitional Employment Demonstration (STED, U.S. Department of Health and Human Services) — a coordinated set of large randomized trials run by MDRC with Abt Associates and MEF Associates — tested the latest generation of public/nonprofit transitional-jobs programs for disadvantaged populations (returning prisoners, long-term TANF recipients) [MDRC2017]. The result is the canonical fade-out pattern: nearly every program produced large employment gains in the first months, but those gains were almost entirely the transitional jobs themselves. Subsidized employment ran at about 99% in the quarter of random assignment, fell to roughly 9% by quarter 4, and to under 1% by quarters 6–9; by quarters 11–14 the program-control difference in employment was statistically insignificant [MDRC2017]. (A few sites showed modest employment and earnings gains in the final follow-up year, after most participants had left the transitional jobs — encouraging but small, and not the across-the-board result a make-work advocate would need.) The lesson is direct: paying people to hold a temporary public job raises employment only while the public job lasts. A subsidy that flows through a private employer facing real product-market discipline, by contrast, is at least attached to a job that may survive the subsidy — which is exactly why the meta-analyses rank private subsidies above public employment, and why a worker-following private subsidy is the better-evidenced instrument.

§4 — Counter-evidence and caveats

A think-tank brief should state the case against its own thesis. Four caveats are serious enough to flag.

1. Publication bias. Meta-analyses are only as good as the studies they aggregate, and a literature that systematically suppresses null results would inflate apparent effectiveness. Card, Kluve, and Weber test for this directly and find no evidence of publication bias: in their 2010 sample, published studies are no more likely than unpublished ones to report significantly positive effects, and 46% of their estimates come from unpublished work precisely to guard against the problem [CardKluveWeber2010]. This is a point in the evidence’s favor, but it is worth noting that the test is for direction of effect, not for the more subtle possibility that the menu of programs chosen for evaluation is itself selected.

2. External validity. Card, Kluve, and Weber’s sample is overwhelmingly European — Germany alone supplies 45 of 199 estimates in 2010, with the Nordic countries and the German-speaking bloc dominating; the “Anglo” countries (including the U.S.) contribute only 20 [CardKluveWeber2010]. European labor markets, with mandatory program assignment, registered-unemployment systems, and stronger employment protection, differ from the U.S. setting in ways that could move effect sizes. Reassuringly, the authors find that once program type and participant composition are controlled, cross-country differences become statistically insignificant — the apparent national differences are mostly compositional [CardKluveWeber2010]. Still, transferring point estimates across institutional contexts is a known hazard, and the U.S. RCT evidence (WIA training; the ETJD/STED transitional jobs) is the more directly relevant check for a U.S. program.

3. Subsidies can carry large deadweight. §3 is candid that deadweight and displacement can be large — 84% deadweight in one French scheme, 65–70% displacement in Sweden [Brown2015]. A subsidy designed badly (broad, open-ended, untargeted, available for all hires) can approach a pure transfer to employers for hires they would have made anyway. The thesis is not that subsidies are unconditionally good; it is that well-designed subsidies are moderately good and that the design levers to get there are known. This makes the empirical case for subsidies conditional on design, which is a meaningfully weaker claim than “subsidies work.”

4. Public employment sometimes pursues non-employment goals. The least-effective verdict on public-sector employment is a verdict on its labor-market return — its ability to raise participants’ subsequent unsubsidized employment and earnings. Public job-creation programs may be defended on other grounds: income support during deep recessions, provision of public goods (infrastructure, care), social inclusion, or maintaining attachment to work among the very hard-to-employ. The evidence reviewed here does not refute those rationales; it shows only that public employment is a poor instrument for the specific objective of durable reemployment. A program whose explicit purpose is to move dislocated workers into lasting private jobs should not choose the instrument that the entire literature ranks last on that objective — but a reader who values public employment for other reasons is not contradicted by this review.

A fifth, more technical caveat deserves mention: most of the underlying studies report almost nothing on costs, so the literature is far richer on “does it raise employment?” than on “is it worth the money?” [CardKluveWeber2010]. The cost-effectiveness ranking — on which JSA’s cheapness is a major advantage and public employment’s expense a major liability — is therefore inferred more than directly measured. This strengthens rather than weakens the case for cheap JSA, but it counsels humility about precise cost-benefit claims for any category.

What this implies for the conceptual motivation and policy design

For the conceptual-motivation section, the international ALMP evidence supplies the empirical spine of the argument that the reflexive policy responses to dislocation are mismatched to the problem. A government job guarantee — the most prominent left-of-center answer to AI displacement — is a subsidized-public-employment program, and that is the single category the entire evaluation literature ranks last, with frequently zero or negative effects on durable reemployment [CardKluveWeber2010; CardKluveWeber2018], a verdict the U.S. transitional-jobs RCTs confirm in the sharpest possible form: employment gains that exist only as long as the public job does [MDRC2017]. “Just create public jobs” is therefore not a neutral default; it is the worst-evidenced option for the stated objective. Conversely, the finding that ALMPs work best for the long-term unemployed and in recessions [CardKluveWeber2018] validates the project’s framing: the population a technology shock strands is the population for whom active policy has the largest measured returns.

For the policy-design section, the evidence points to a specific architecture rather than a slogan:

  • Lead with private-employer subsidies plus job-search assistance, not public make-work. The ranking favors a worker-following private-sector employment subsidy (moderately effective, and attached to a job that can outlast the subsidy) paired with cheap, fast JSA (the short-run win) over government job creation (the laggard) [CardKluveWeber2010; CardKluveWeber2018; MDRC2017].
  • Pair the subsidy with training to capture the medium-run return. Because training’s payoff is lagged but real [CardKluveWeber2010], embedding employer-delivered, on-the-job training in the subsidized placement — rather than offering detached classroom vouchers — lets the program collect the human-capital return while avoiding the lock-in that makes stand-alone training look weak in the short run. (This dovetails with Review 03’s RCT finding that employer-linked, job-attached training is the variety that actually pays off.)
  • Engineer against deadweight, substitution, and displacement. Because the subsidy case is conditional on design, the program must build in the levers the evidence identifies: target the long-term unemployed and the genuinely dislocated (low counterfactual hire rates, low deadweight); condition payment on net new hires and on retention past the subsidy window (so the policy buys lasting jobs, not churn); time-limit the subsidy; and — to address the dislocation framing specifically — attach demand-contingent triggers so subsidies activate where and when labor demand has actually fallen, rather than flowing as an unconditional employer transfer [Brown2015; OECD2025].

The through-line is that the evidence does not merely permit a private-subsidy-plus-JSA design; it recommends it over the alternatives, and it specifies the design conditions under which that recommendation holds. A technology adjustment program that ignored those conditions — an untargeted, open-ended subsidy, or worse a public job guarantee — would be choosing instruments the literature has already graded, and grading poorly.

§2 — Core subsidy mechanism

What the subsidy pays for. The program reimburses employers (public sector, private for-profit, private non-profit, or social enterprise) for placing an eligible individual in a subsidized job. The reimbursable “employer subsidies” are explicitly enumerated as: [SenateFinanceSxS]

  1. wages;
  2. the employer share of payroll taxes;
  3. employer costs for unemployment insurance;
  4. employer costs for workers’ compensation premiums; and
  5. costs attributable to supervision and other administration directly related to the employment of the eligible individual in the subsidized placement.

The 120% cap. “A public or private sector employer is not eligible to be paid a subsidy that exceeds 120 percent of wage costs.” [SenateFinanceSxS] The extra 20 percentage points above bare wages is what covers the payroll-tax/UI/workers’-comp/supervision-and-admin bundle. Niskanen frames this as employers being “made whole” for the full cost of hiring plus a margin. [Niskanen2025]

CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE (subsidy flows to the EMPLOYER). In ELEVATE the subsidy is paid to the employer, not the worker. The bill conceives of this as a labor-demand instrument — Niskanen explicitly contrasts it with wage subsidies like the EITC, which boost worker post-tax supply-side earnings; ELEVATE instead directly defrays employer hiring costs to create demand for the targeted worker. Our remade program reframes this as a worker-following benefit (the support attaches to the displaced/affected worker and is portable across employers). Document the original faithfully here; the divergence is logged in §10. [SenateFinanceSxS] [Niskanen2025]

Worker pay & protections. “An eligible individual in a subsidized job placement cannot be paid less than the prevailing minimum wage and [must] be covered by all relevant employment and labor laws in the jurisdiction.” So subsidized workers are real employees with full labor-law coverage, not sub-minimum trainees. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Placement duration. “A subsidized job placement for an eligible individual cannot exceed six months unless the placement is extended to accommodate time needed for training.” The six-month cap may also be waived in periods of high unemployment. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Permitted use of the subsidy. Subsidies may be used by the employer only for “costs attributable to the hiring of, compensation for, or on-the-job training of, the eligible individual.” [SenateFinanceSxS]

Conversion to unsubsidized work. Employers “must make a good faith effort to hire the individual” into an unsubsidized job if the worker has “demonstrated satisfactory performance and there is an available job.” Near the end of the subsidy period, the worker is re-assessed to decide whether to stay with the employer or receive additional services. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Re-eligibility rule. “If an eligible individual is unemployed for at least six weeks after completing a transitional job placement, the eligible individual may apply for a new subsidized job placement but only with a different public or private sector employer.” The “different employer” constraint is an anti-churn / anti-capture device — it prevents an employer from cycling the same worker through repeated subsidized stints. [SenateFinanceSxS]

§3 — Worker eligibility & enrollment

Core eligibility test. Eligible individuals are those “assessed as unlikely to find regular, [un]subsidized employment due to” one of three triggers: [SenateFinanceSxS]

  1. being an individual with barriers to employment (using the WIOA definition);
  2. being a dislocated worker; or
  3. local or national economic conditions.

The third trigger is the macro/counter-cyclical hook — it lets the program scale into a community where the problem is the labor market, not the individual.

Target populations (enumerated). The statute and summary name “economically and socially disadvantaged” populations including: long-term unemployed, people with criminal records (formerly incarcerated), former foster youth, and (per the broader program description and Niskanen) current/former/eligible SNAP and TANF recipients, noncustodial parents under child-support orders, and homeless individuals. [SenateFinanceSxS] [Niskanen2025]

States choose which populations to serve. State plans must specify “[w]hich specific populations of eligible individuals the state will serve, with a focus on how the state plans to assess and serve the eligible individuals with serious or multiple barriers to employment.” This is deliberate flexibility — states tailor to “community-specific workforce gaps and employer needs.” [SenateFinanceSxS]

Assessment + employability plan (the 30-day clock). States must, “within 30 days of the eligible individual commencing the state program,” assess the individual’s “job skills, education, past work experience, and potential barriers to employment,” and then create an individualized employability plan specifying the worker’s “ultimate employment goal” and the services/training that will get them there. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Supportive services. Defined consistent with WIOA plus additions: stipends for those in unpaid training, legal services, and other supplemental services; child/dependent care and transportation are explicitly included as supports “necessary to enable an individual to participate in employment activities.” [SenateFinanceSxS]

§4 — Employer screening & training obligations

On-the-job training. Subsidy dollars are explicitly permitted for “on-the-job training of” the eligible individual, embedding training inside the placement rather than as a separate classroom track. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Good-faith retention effort. As noted in §2, participating employers “must make a good faith effort to hire the individual” into unsubsidized work given satisfactory performance and an available position. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Non-displacement rules (anti-abuse core). An employer “cannot use an eligible individual subsidized job placement to displace an employee, position, or volunteer, or to partially displace any employee, position, or volunteer such as through a reduction in hours, wages, or employment benefits” — and explicitly cannot displace a worker participating in a strike or union activities. [SenateFinanceSxS] These are standard subsidized-employment guardrails to prevent windfall substitution of subsidized for incumbent workers and to keep the program from being used as strike-breaking.

“Pro-Worker Employer” demonstration (the good-job criteria). Section 2 authorizes a demonstration authority under which states, local governments, and tribes/tribal consortia may test “innovative strategies to promote the identification of and subsidized job placement with ‘Pro-Worker Employers.’” The bill establishes a standards-and-certification process; a Pro-Worker Employer must at a minimum: [SenateFinanceSxS]

  1. Offer predictability — scheduling practices with predictable work hours.
  2. Provide family-friendly benefits — paid family and medical leave, paid sick days, health insurance, child care, paid time off, and benefits supporting work-life balance, including seeking to extend similar/portable benefits to freelance, independent-contractor, and other contingent workers.
  3. Ensure adequate pay — a living wage sufficient to meet basic needs, plus fair-compensation policies (clear contracts, on-time payment) for contingent workers.
  4. Invest in employee growth and development — provide (in-house or via stipends) access to education and training and career pathways for entry-level workers that demonstrably improve income and responsibilities.
  5. Reward workers when the company does well — profit-sharing or other mechanisms so workers benefit when the firm grows.

This is the bill’s articulated “good job” standard, framed as an aspirational/demonstration tier rather than a universal requirement. [SenateFinanceSxS] [Niskanen2025]

§5 — Federal–state delivery architecture

Statutory vehicle. The program amends the Social Security Act to add a new Title XIII — “Reemployment and Other Job Related Assistance and Benefits” — with Subpart A = “Employment, Training and Supportive Services Program” (the subsidized-jobs core, Section 2) and Part B = self-employment and relocation benefits (Section 3, see §8). [SenateFinanceSxS]

Who approves. State plans are approved by the Secretary of Health and Human Services (HHS), “in consultation with the Secretaries of the Departments of Labor and Education.” [SenateFinanceSxS] Note: ELEVATE deliberately routes the program through HHS, not DOL — a notable institutional choice given that workforce programs (WIOA) are DOL-administered.

WIOA alignment. State plans must be “submitted at the same time and in the same manner as the state WIOA plan,” must explain how the strategy “fits with the state’s overall … workforce needs,” and must coordinate with WIOA and other systems (criminal justice, child support, child welfare, homeless services). HHS must also “coordinate with the Secretary of Labor on aligning performance measures and regulations” with WIOA, and harmonize data collection/evaluation with WIOA where possible. Plans must be submitted at least 90 days before a state begins operating. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Spending guardrails. [SenateFinanceSxS]

  • ≥70% on direct employment services — “a minimum of 70 percent of funds paid to each state [must] be directed toward direct employment services,” defined as employer wage costs, the employer share of payroll and unemployment taxes, and workers’-comp premiums.
  • ≤15% administrative — “Caps administrative expenses at 15 percent.”
  • Maintenance of effort (MOE) — total state spending on employment/training services “through programs such as WIOA, shall not be less than … fiscal year 2017.” (FY2017 is the verified baseline year.)
  • Supplement-not-supplant — states must verify new funding “will supplement not supplant existing state expenditures.”

Planning grants. Two years of grant funding “(at such sums as are necessary), with no matching requirement” for planning, implementation, and technical assistance. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Direct funding for tribes and local governments. [SenateFinanceSxS]

  • Tribes — any tribe or consortium operating a TANF program may receive direct funding from HHS and a federal match at the tribal FMAP rate.
  • Local governments — if a state declines to establish a program, HHS must establish procedures for a local government (or consortium) to run one directly.

Oversight. Quarterly state reports to HHS; annual HHS reports to Congress on performance, populations, and activities; new HHS performance measures targeting income gains and poverty reduction; and a biennial HHS Inspector General audit of a sample of state programs for waste/fraud/abuse. [SenateFinanceSxS]

§6 — Three-tier evidence framework

ELEVATE requires states to use evidence-based practices graded into three tiers; states “may only receive funds for training, services, and supports that directly result in employment.” [SenateFinanceSxS]

Tier-1 (weakest — promising / logic-model). Practices that: offer a new or potentially more effective strategy for widely shared challenges; have not been systematically studied but are supported by a strong logic model; and depart from existing workforce strategies. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Tier-2 (middle — evaluated with some positive results, or strong quasi-experimental). Practices that either: (a) have been implemented by a state program and evaluated, with results indicating “some potential for positive impacts”; or (b) have not been previously implemented by the state but are supported by strong evidence, evaluated using a quasi-experimental or random-assignment design, with findings indicating “some statistically significant positive change.” [SenateFinanceSxS]

Tier-3 (strongest — random-assignment positive impacts). Practices that: have previously been implemented by a state-funded program; have previously been evaluated using a random-assignment design with findings indicating “some positive impacts”; and support “a significant expansion of structural or service delivery reform ideas.” [SenateFinanceSxS]

The ramp-up rule. “Beginning with the 21st fiscal year quarter for which the state carries out the program, at least 50 percent of the total amount expended by the state must be for … a Tier-2 or Tier-3 practice.” [SenateFinanceSxS]

Verified detail. The PDF says “21st fiscal year quarter” (i.e., the 21st quarter of operation — roughly year 6), not “21st fiscal quarter” in any calendar sense. The 50% Tier-2/Tier-3 floor phases in after a multi-year runway so states can build the evidence base before being held to it. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Downturn suspension. Both the evidence-tier requirement generally and the 50% Tier-2/Tier-3 floor are suspended during economic downturns — “These criteria may be waived in an economic downturn” / “This criteria is also suspended during downturns.” The logic: in a recession the priority is volume of placements, not evidentiary purity. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Evaluation cost-share. States may receive funding to run rigorous evaluations if HHS approves the design and “[u]nless waived by the Secretary, the state contributes … from non-federal sources, an amount equal to at least 25 percent of the cost of the proposed evaluation.” So the 25% non-federal cost share is waivable and applies specifically to evaluations (not to program spending generally). [SenateFinanceSxS]

Performance-incentive study. HHS must contract with the National Academy of Sciences to study the relationship between engagement, impacts, and outcomes, and to recommend to Congress “whether and how program performance might be tied to fiscal incentives.” [SenateFinanceSxS]

§7 — Automatic stabilizers (FMAP-style)

Baseline match. In normal times, states receive the federal medical assistance percentage (FMAP) — the same income-indexed Medicaid match formula — on “the total amount expended by the state during the quarter” for employment/training services and administration. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Counter-cyclical ramp. The federal share rises with total unemployment, “up to a maximum of full federal funding in severe downturns.” If total unemployment in a quarter is: [SenateFinanceSxS]

Total unemployment (quarter) Federal share
≥ 6.5% and < 7% FMAP + 20 pts
≥ 7% and < 7.5% FMAP + 30 pts
≥ 7.5% and < 8% FMAP + 40 pts
≥ 8% FMAP + 50 pts or 100, whichever is lesser

VERIFIED DISCREPANCY — “lesser” vs. “greater.” The primary PDF reads, verbatim: “equal to or greater than 8 percent, the federal share is FMAP + 50 pts or 100, whichever is lesser.” The Niskanen explainer describes the top tier as “FMAP + 50 percentage points or 100% … whichever is greater.” The PDF controls (per instructions): the operative word is “lesser.” Mechanically, “lesser” is the only one that makes arithmetic sense as a ceiling — it caps the federal share at 100% (since FMAP+50 could otherwise exceed 100% for high-FMAP states, e.g., a 70% FMAP + 50 = 120%, which is nonsensical; “lesser of FMAP+50 or 100” correctly clamps it to 100%). Niskanen’s “greater” appears to be loose paraphrase. Flag this whenever the stabilizer is discussed. [SenateFinanceSxS] [Niskanen2025]

Why it matters (design intent). The FMAP ramp is the heart of ELEVATE’s “job guarantee that can work” pitch: spending and federal cost-share automatically expand in recessions (when subsidized jobs are most needed and the evidence/tier rules are suspended) and contract in good times (when private demand absorbs workers). It converts a discretionary program into a quasi-automatic stabilizer without requiring new appropriations in each downturn. [SenateFinanceSxS] [Niskanen2025]

§8 — Relocation & self-employment benefits

Section 3 establishes Title XIII, Part B — two ancillary benefits, both administered by the Social Security Administration (SSA) in consultation with the Department of Labor, both exempt from federal taxation, and both housed in a new Office of Reemployment Assistance within SSA. Applications may be filed online, at SSA field offices, through WIOA one-stop centers, or at SBA offices. Existing Social Security overpayment/underpayment and fraud-penalty processes apply. [SenateFinanceSxS]

Relocation Assistance Benefit. A maximum benefit of $2,000 (“which can be adjusted upwards to account for family size”), covering up to 90 percent of the reasonable and necessary expenses of relocating the individual, their family, and household effects — “including subsistence and transportation expenses.” Eligibility: dislocated workers, long-term unemployed, or underemployed individuals who have “obtained a suitable offer of work or have a reasonable expectation of obtaining such work in a new geographic region.” [SenateFinanceSxS]

  • Evidence base (per Niskanen). Niskanen analogizes the benefit to military relocation assistance and cites international evidence: a German relocation-assistance evaluation found participants “earn significantly higher wages and find more stable jobs,” and an Australian program (relocation grants on the order of A$3,000–6,000) found a majority of participants remained off income support 12 months after relocating. [Niskanen2025]

Self-Employment Assistance Benefit. Eligible individuals receive a benefit “equal to half their prior earnings up to a cap of the unemployment insurance benefit cap in the state … for a maximum of 26 weeks.” The purpose is to let recently-unemployed workers “work full time on starting their own business.” Eligibility: those who are (a) eligible for regular UI, or (b) ineligible for UI but unemployed “in the most recent 12 weeks through no fault of their own,” or (c) were self-employed with sufficient earnings but lost a hiring contract — and who have “a viable business plan as determined by their state Department of Labor, workforce board, or Small Business Administration.” Concurrent receipt of UI and self-employment assistance is prohibited. [SenateFinanceSxS]

§9 — Cost / CBO logic

No verified CBO score for ELEVATE itself. Niskanen states that because ELEVATE’s outlays “vary dramatically depending on the state of the business cycle,” the CBO modeling required to score it was still being developed — i.e., there is no published CBO cost estimate for S.136. Do not attribute any specific score to the bill. [Niskanen2025]

The $16B/yr comparable. Niskanen references “an earlier proposal for a national subsidized employment program [that] estimated total costs of $16 billion a year.” This is a comparable / order-of-magnitude reference point, not a score of ELEVATE. Treat it as illustrative only. [Niskanen2025]

ALMP-gap framing. Niskanen situates the program against the U.S.’s thin active-labor-market-policy spending — roughly 0.11% of GDP — noting it would take on the order of ~$24 billion/year of additional ALMP spending for the U.S. to “catch up with other Anglo-American countries.” This is context for why a program like ELEVATE is “affordable” relative to peer norms, not a cost estimate of the bill. [Niskanen2025]

Cost behavior (design logic). The intended budget signature is counter-cyclical: minimal outlays in good times (few people qualify under the macro trigger; the FMAP ramp stays at baseline), ramping sharply in recessions (the macro trigger and FMAP bonuses kick in, evidence-tier limits are suspended, placement caps are waivable). This is the same logic that makes it hard to score statically. [SenateFinanceSxS] [Niskanen2025]

§10 — What we keep, change, or drop for the remade program

Mapping each ELEVATE feature to a design decision for our original “technology adjustment assistance” framework. (Reminder: the published work must never name ELEVATE; these are internal design notes.)

# ELEVATE feature Decision Rationale / how we remake it
1 Subsidy paid to the EMPLOYER (labor-demand instrument), capped at 120% of wage costs CHANGE — reframe as worker-following Make the benefit attach to the displaced/affected worker and be portable across employers. Keeps the “make-whole” economics but reframes the political/conceptual story around the worker harmed by AI displacement, not the firm. Retain a cap analogous to 120% of wage costs to fund payroll-tax/UI/comp/supervision bundle.
2 Eligibility triggers: WIOA barriers / dislocated worker / local-or-national economic conditions CHANGE + ADD Add an AI-era trigger: eligibility keyed to technology-driven displacement / unmet-demand conditions (sector/occupation exposure to automation, local AI-shock indicators). Keep “dislocated worker” and the macro condition; drop the welfare-population framing as the primary gate.
3 Target populations (SNAP/TANF/foster/incarcerated/homeless/noncustodial) CHANGE Reorient from a disadvantage-targeting program to a displacement-targeting program. Disadvantaged populations can remain a secondary priority lens, but the spine should be technology adjustment, not anti-poverty.
4 6-month placement cap, extendable for training, waivable in high unemployment KEEP (adapt) Time-limited transitional placement is sound. Keep extension-for-training and downturn waivers; consider an AI-shock waiver analogue.
5 Re-eligibility after 6 weeks unemployed, only with a different employer KEEP Clean anti-churn / anti-capture device; preserve directly.
6 Minimum-wage floor + full labor-law coverage KEEP Non-negotiable worker-protection baseline; preserve.
7 Non-displacement rules (no displacing/partial-displacing incumbents; no strike-breaking) KEEP Essential anti-abuse guardrails; preserve verbatim in substance.
8 “Pro-Worker Employer” good-job criteria (predictable scheduling, paid leave/sick/health/childcare, living pay, growth/career pathways, profit-sharing) KEEP Strong, well-articulated “good job” standard. Preserve as our quality bar for participating employers — possibly elevate from demonstration to a stronger requirement.
9 On-the-job training + good-faith retention effort KEEP Embedding training in placement and requiring conversion-to-unsubsidized is the conversion engine; preserve.
10 Employee Retention Credit: 40% of 2nd-year wages, capped $6,000, for 2-yr retention (Sec. 4) KEEP (revisit cap) Useful conversion incentive. Keep the mechanism; revisit the $6,000 cap and the 40%/second-year structure for an AI-era wage distribution.
11 Delivery via new SSA-Act Title XIII; HHS approves plans (consulting DOL & Education) CHANGE — reconsider lead agency HHS-led is an odd fit for a workforce program. Consider DOL-led (closer to WIOA/TAA) or a dedicated office. Keep the inter-agency consultation structure.
12 WIOA alignment (co-submitted plans, aligned performance measures/data) KEEP Integration with existing workforce infrastructure avoids a parallel bureaucracy; preserve.
13 Guardrails: ≥70% direct employment, ≤15% admin, MOE vs FY2017, supplement-not-supplant KEEP (refresh baseline) Preserve all four; update the MOE baseline year from FY2017 to a current year.
14 Tribes & local governments can receive direct funding; local backstop if state opts out KEEP Good federalism/backstop design; preserve.
15 Three evidence tiers (1 logic-model / 2 evaluated-or-strong-QE / 3 RCT-positive); ≥50% Tier-2/3 from 21st quarter; downturn suspension KEEP Strong, defensible evidence regime. Preserve the tiers, the phase-in runway, and the downturn suspension.
16 Evaluation cost-share: ≥25% non-federal, waivable; NAS performance study KEEP Preserve; keeps states invested in rigorous evaluation.
17 FMAP-style automatic stabilizers (ramp at 6.5/7/7.5/8% unemployment to 100%) KEEP — flagship feature This is the single most valuable mechanism to carry forward. Preserve the FMAP ramp; use the corrected “lesser of FMAP+50 or 100” ceiling wording. Consider whether AI-displacement indices should also trigger the ramp, not just headline unemployment.
18 Relocation subsidy ($2,000, family-size adjustable, ≤90% of moving costs, SSA-administered, tax-free) KEEP Strong mobility tool with real international evidence (German/Australian). Preserve; revisit the $2,000 amount.
19 Self-employment benefit (½ prior earnings up to state UI cap, ≤26 weeks, viable-business-plan gate) KEEP Preserve; complements the “start something new” path for displaced workers.
20 Counter-cyclical cost profile / no static CBO score KEEP (as feature, plan for scoring) Preserve the counter-cyclical design but anticipate the scoring difficulty — build in the modeling assumptions up front so our version is scoreable.

Net summary of the remake. Keep the machinery — FMAP automatic stabilizers, evidence tiers, good-job/”Pro-Worker” criteria, non-displacement guardrails, relocation and self-employment benefits, WIOA integration, the retention credit, the 70/15/MOE guardrails. Change the framing and the entry door: (a) flip the subsidy from employer-following to worker-following/portable; (b) replace the disadvantage-population targeting with an AI-era technology-displacement / unmet-demand eligibility trigger; (c) reconsider HHS-as-lead in favor of a workforce-native agency; (d) refresh the MOE baseline. The result is an original “technology adjustment assistance” program that owes its mechanics — but not its name, framing, or statutory home — to this bill.
